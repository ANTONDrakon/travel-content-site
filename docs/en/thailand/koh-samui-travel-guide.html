Koh Samui Travel Guide 2026

Koh Samui, Thailand’s second-largest island after Phuket, spans 228.7 square kilometers in the Gulf of Thailand. Once a quiet coconut-farming community, it has evolved into a world-class destination that still retains pockets of authentic tropical charm. The island is defined by its palm-fringed beaches, lush mountainous interior, and a laid-back atmosphere that shifts from serene coves in the north to vibrant party strips in the east. The air is thick with the scent of frangipani and salt, and the pace of life slows to the rhythm of the tides.

Why visit Koh Samui in 2026? The island offers an unmatched diversity of experiences within a compact area. You can spend mornings snorkeling among coral reefs at Ang Thong Marine Park, afternoons exploring waterfalls hidden in the jungle, and evenings dining on freshly caught seafood under the stars. The infrastructure has matured significantly, with modern hospitals, international-standard spas, and reliable transport, yet the local culture remains strong in fishing villages like Bophut and the iconic Big Buddha temple. Average temperatures hover around 28°C year-round, making it a perpetual summer escape.

Beyond the beaches, Koh Samui is a gateway to wellness retreats, Muay Thai training camps, and culinary adventures. The island hosts the Samui Regatta in May and the Full Moon Party’s quieter cousin, the Half Moon Party, on nearby Koh Phangan. With direct flights from over 20 international cities and a growing eco-tourism focus, 2026 is an ideal year to discover why this island consistently ranks among the top tropical destinations in Southeast Asia.

How to Get There

Koh Samui International Airport (USM) is the primary gateway, located about 2 kilometers north of Chaweng Beach. The airport is privately owned by Bangkok Airways, which operates the vast majority of flights. In 2026, the airport handles approximately 2.8 million passengers annually, with direct flights from Bangkok (1 hour 15 minutes), Singapore (2 hours), Kuala Lumpur (1 hour 45 minutes), Hong Kong (3 hours 30 minutes), and seasonal routes from Shanghai, Seoul, and Dubai.

Average flight prices in 2026: Bangkok to Koh Samui round-trip costs between $120 and $250 on Bangkok Airways, with Air Asia offering limited connections via Surat Thani. From Singapore, expect $180 to $350 round-trip. From Europe, budget $600 to $1,000 for a round-trip with one stop in Bangkok. During peak season (December to February), prices increase by 30–50%. For budget travelers, fly to Surat Thani Airport (URT) on the mainland and take a 90-minute ferry (Raja Ferry or Lomprayah) for $15–$25, arriving at Nathon Pier.

Top 15 Attractions

  1. Big Buddha Temple (Wat Phra Yai) – A 12-meter-tall golden Buddha statue on a small island connected by a causeway. Free entry, open daily 6:00 AM–6:00 PM. Donations accepted.
  2. Ang Thong National Marine Park – An archipelago of 42 islands accessible by speedboat or kayak. Entry fee: $10 for adults, $5 for children. Open 8:00 AM–5:00 PM. Tours cost $40–$80.
  3. Na Muang Waterfall 1 & 2 – Two waterfalls in the jungle. Na Muang 1 is 18 meters high with a swimming pool; Na Muang 2 is 80 meters high. Free entry, open 8:00 AM–5:00 PM.
  4. Wat Plai Laem – A colorful Chinese-Thai temple with a 18-armed Guanyin statue. Free entry, open 8:00 AM–6:00 PM.
  5. Chaweng Beach – The island’s longest beach at 7 kilometers. Free access, with sunbeds for rent at $3–$5 per day.
  6. Lamai Beach – A 4-kilometer stretch with rock formations called Hin Ta and Hin Yai. Free entry.
  7. Secret Buddha Garden – A hillside garden filled with statues and sculptures. Entry fee: $5. Open 8:00 AM–5:00 PM.
  8. Samui Elephant Sanctuary – An ethical sanctuary for rescued elephants. Entry fee: $50 for adults, $25 for children. Open 8:00 AM–4:00 PM.
  9. Grandfather and Grandmother Rocks (Hin Ta Hin Yai) – Natural rock formations resembling male and female genitalia. Free entry, open 24 hours.
  10. Fisherman’s Village Walking Street – A night market every Friday evening in Bophut. Free entry, food stalls with dishes from $1–$5.
  11. Samui Water Park Pink Elephant – A family-friendly water park with slides and pools. Entry fee: $15 for adults, $10 for children. Open 10:00 AM–6:00 PM.
  12. Koh Tao Snorkeling Day Trip – A full-day trip to the nearby island known for crystal-clear water and coral reefs. Cost: $40–$70 including lunch and equipment.
  13. Samui Crocodile Farm – A small zoo with crocodile shows. Entry fee: $5. Open 9:00 AM–6:00 PM.
  14. Overlap Stone – A dramatic viewpoint near Lamai with huge boulders. Free entry, open 6:00 AM–6:00 PM.
  15. Magic Alambic Rum Distillery – A craft rum distillery offering tours and tastings. Tour fee: $10 including samples. Open 10:00 AM–6:00 PM.

Getting Around

Koh Samui has no metro or train system. The primary modes of transport are songthaews (shared pickup trucks), taxis, motorbike rentals, and private car hires.

  • Songthaews: Fixed-route trucks running along the ring road. Fares are $1–$3 per trip, depending on distance. They run from 6:00 AM to 10:00 PM.
  • Taxis: Metered taxis are rare; most negotiate flat fares. Short trips (e.g., Chaweng to Lamai) cost $10–$15. Longer trips (airport to Maenam) cost $20–$30. Always agree on price before boarding.
  • Motorbike Rental: The most popular option. Rentals cost $5–$15 per day for a 125cc scooter, $20–$40 per day for larger bikes. Requires a valid international driving permit. Helmets are mandatory and included.
  • Car Rental: Compact cars from $25–$50 per day, SUVs from $60–$100 per day. Rent from reliable agencies like Budget or Hertz at the airport.
  • Private Driver: Hire a driver for a full day (8 hours) for $50–$80, ideal for sightseeing.
  • Ferries: For island hopping, passenger ferries to Koh Phangan ($10–$15) and Koh Tao ($20–$30) depart from Nathon and Bangrak piers.

Where to Eat

Koh Samui’s culinary scene ranges from street food stalls to Michelin-starred restaurants. Local cuisine emphasizes fresh seafood, coconut, and tropical fruits.

  • Street Food: Try pad thai, som tam (papaya salad), and grilled satay at night markets. Prices: $1–$3 per dish. Fisherman’s Village Walking Street and Lamai Night Market are top spots.
  • Local Restaurants: Eat at family-run places like Sujinda Restaurant in Maenam (curries from $4–$8) or Krua Bophut (seafood platter for $10–$15).
  • Fine Dining: The Dining Room at Six Senses Samui offers tasting menus from $60–$100 per person. Dara Serene Restaurant in Chaweng serves Thai fusion with ocean views, mains $15–$30.
  • Seafood: Visit The Jungle Club for grilled fish and coconut rice, mains $8–$15. For casual beachfront dining, The Hut in Chaweng Noi has whole snapper for $12.
  • Coffee and Desserts: Samui has a growing café culture. Popular spots include The Coffee Club in Chaweng (espresso $3) and Mango Bay Café (mango sticky rice $4).

Visa & Documents

Koh Samui is part of Thailand. Citizens of 64 countries, including the United States, United Kingdom, Australia, Canada, and most European Union nations, enjoy visa-free entry for up to 30 days when arriving by air. For land or sea arrivals, the visa-free period is 15 days. Your passport must be valid for at least six months from the date of entry.

For longer stays, apply for a 60-day Tourist Visa (single entry) at a Thai embassy or consulate before travel, costing $30–$40. Extensions of stay (30 days) are available at the Samui Immigration Office in Nathon for $50. Overstaying incurs a fine of $1 per day. No vaccination is required for entry, but routine vaccines (hepatitis A, typhoid) are recommended.

10 Travel Tips

  1. Rent a motorbike with caution: Roads can be steep and slippery, especially after rain. Always wear a helmet and carry your international driving permit. Police checks are common, and fines for no license are $10–$15.
  2. Bargain respectfully: Negotiate prices at markets and for taxis, but do so with a smile. A 10–20% discount is reasonable.
  3. Protect against mosquitoes: Dengue fever is present. Use DEET repellent, especially during dawn and dusk. Consider mosquito nets if staying in budget accommodation.
  4. Carry cash: Many street stalls and smaller shops do not accept credit cards. ATMs charge a fee of $2–$3 per withdrawal.
  5. Stay hydrated: Buy bottled water (30–50 cents per liter) or use a reusable bottle with purification tablets. Tap water is not drinkable.
  6. Respect temple etiquette: Cover shoulders and knees when visiting temples. Remove shoes before entering temple buildings. Do not touch Buddha images.
  7. Book tours through reputable operators: For Ang Thong Marine Park or snorkeling trips, use licensed companies like Lomprayah or Sea Star. Check reviews on TripAdvisor or Google.
