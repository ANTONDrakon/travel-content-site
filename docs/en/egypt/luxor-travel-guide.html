Luxor Travel Guide 2026

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Luxor, often called the world’s greatest open-air museum, is a city in southern Egypt that straddles the Nile River. The city is divided into two distinct parts: the East Bank, home to the Luxor Temple and the Karnak Temple Complex, and the West Bank, where the Valley of the Kings, Valley of the Queens, and the Temple of Hatshepsut are carved into the desert mountains. The atmosphere is a blend of ancient grandeur and modern Egyptian life, with horse-drawn carriages (caleches) clattering alongside cars, and the call to prayer echoing over the ruins. Over 60% of Egypt’s ancient monuments are located in or near Luxor, making it an unparalleled destination for history enthusiasts.

Why is 2026 the year to visit? The city has undergone a major renovation of the Avenue of Sphinxes, a 2.7-kilometer pathway connecting Luxor Temple to Karnak Temple, which was fully reopened in 2021 and now hosts nightly sound-and-light shows. Additionally, the Grand Egyptian Museum (GEM) near Cairo is now fully open, but Luxor remains the core for in-situ exploration—you can walk through tombs that are 3,500 years old. The local tourism infrastructure has improved, with more boutique hotels and guided tours offering sustainable practices. The weather is reliably sunny, with over 340 days of sunshine per year, making it a year-round destination for archaeology, photography, and river cruises.

Luxor is also a gateway to understanding ancient Egyptian cosmology. The temples are aligned with the winter solstice and the Nile’s annual flood cycles. In 2026, expect fewer crowds than in previous years due to global travel shifts, but still significant numbers from Europe and Asia. The city’s population is about 500,000, but it receives over 3 million tourists annually. The best approach is to spend at least three full days: one for the East Bank, one for the West Bank, and one for a hot air balloon ride over the Valley of the Kings at dawn.

How to Get There

Luxor International Airport (LXR) is located about 6 kilometers east of the city center. It serves direct flights from major hubs. For example, EgyptAir operates daily flights from Cairo (1 hour 10 minutes, average round-trip $150–$250). Turkish Airlines flies from Istanbul (2 hours 45 minutes, round-trip $300–$500). From London (LHR), British Airways offers seasonal direct flights (5 hours, round-trip $400–$700). From Paris (CDG), Air France flies direct (4 hours 30 minutes, round-trip $350–$600). From Dubai (DXB), Emirates has daily flights (3 hours 45 minutes, round-trip $400–$650). Average ticket prices in 2026 are expected to be 10–15% higher than 2023 due to inflation, but advance booking (3 months ahead) can save up to 30%.

Alternatively, you can fly into Cairo (CAI) and take a domestic flight or a night train. The sleeper train from Cairo to Luxor (Wagons-Lits) costs about $80–$120 per person for a two-berth cabin, takes 10–12 hours, and includes dinner and breakfast. This is a popular option for those wanting to save a night’s accommodation.

Where to Stay

Luxor’s accommodation ranges from budget hostels to world-class resorts. The main areas are the East Bank (convenient for temples and restaurants) and the West Bank (quieter, closer to tombs, with stunning desert views).

East Bank

  • Budget ($15–$40/night): Bob Marley Peace Hotel (dorm beds $15 private rooms $30). Located near Luxor Temple. Basic but clean, with a rooftop terrace.
  • Mid-Range ($50–$120/night): Steigenberger Nile Palace (4-star, double rooms $90–$120). Includes pool, spa, and a Nile view. Also the Mercure Luxor Karnak (5-star, doubles $80–$110).
  • Luxury ($150–$400/night): Sofitel Winter Palace Luxor (historic 5-star, doubles $200–$350). Built in 1886, with gardens and a famous bar. Also Hilton Luxor Resort & Spa (5-star, doubles $180–$300).

West Bank

  • Budget ($20–$50/night): Al Moudira Hotel (boutique, doubles $50–$80). A charming villa-style hotel with gardens and a pool. Also many local guesthouses near the Valley of the Kings for $25–$40
  • Mid-Range ($60–$130/night): Marsam Hotel (doubles $70–$100). Run by the same family for decades, simple but authentic, with views of the Theban Hills.
  • Luxury ($150–$300/night): Pavillon Winter Luxor (part of Sofitel, doubles $150–$250). More modern than the main Winter Palace, with a large pool.

Getting Around

Luxor has no metro system. The main modes of transport are taxis, buses, and boats.

  • Taxis: White taxis with blue stripes. No meters; negotiate before riding. Short trips within East Bank: 20–$1 (40 EGP) ($0–$1). From East Bank to West Bank (via bridge or ferry): 50–$2 (100 EGP) ($1–$2). Full-day private hire: 400–$16 (800 EGP) ($8–$16).
  • Motorized Rickshaws (Tuk-tuks): Common for short distances. 10–$0 (20 EGP) per ride. Negotiate.
  • Public Buses: Limited and crowded. Route 1 runs along the Corniche (riverfront) for$0 (5 EGP). Not recommended for tourists.
  • Ferry (Motorboat): Cross the Nile from East Bank (Luxor Temple area) to West Bank (near Valley of the Kings). 5–$0 (10 EGP) per person. Runs 6:00 AM–9:00 PM.
  • Car Rental: Available at the airport or hotels. Expect $30–$50 per day for a small car. Requires an International Driving Permit. Parking is limited near temples.
  • Bicycle: Rent for $5–$10 per day. Good for the West Bank, but roads can be dusty.

Where to Eat

Luxor offers a mix of Egyptian staples (koshari, ful medames, taameya) and international cuisine. Most restaurants add a 12% service charge.

Street Food & Budget ($1–$5 per meal)

  • Koshari El Tahrir (East Bank): Classic Egyptian dish of rice, lentils, pasta, chickpeas, and tomato sauce. Plate:$0 (25 EGP) ($0).
  • Foul & Taameya (various stalls near Luxor Temple): Fava bean stew (ful) and falafel (taameya) sandwiches. 10–$0 (15 EGP) each.
  • Sofra Restaurant & Café (East Bank): Mid-range but affordable lunch menu: 100–$3 (150 EGP) for a full meal of grilled meats and salads.

Mid-Range ($10–$25 per meal)

  • El Kababgy (East Bank, near Winter Palace): Egyptian and Middle Eastern grill. Mixed grill plate:$4 (200 EGP) ($4).
  • Marsam Hotel Restaurant (West Bank): Simple rooftop dining with views of the Theban Hills. Stuffed pigeon (hamam):$3 (150 EGP).
  • Al Moudira Hotel Restaurant (West Bank): Garden setting. International and Egyptian menu. Main courses: 250–$8 (400 EGP).

Fine Dining ($30–$60 per meal)

  • 1886 Restaurant (Sofitel Winter Palace): Fine French-Egyptian fusion. Tasting menu:$24 (1 $4 (200 EGP)) ($24).
  • Nile Ritz-Carlton (East Bank): Rooftop dining with Nile views. Steak or seafood: 800–$30 (1 $10 (500 EGP)).
  • Moon Valley (West Bank): Set menu with traditional Egyptian dishes in a Bedouin-style tent. 500–$16 (800 EGP).

Best Time to Visit

Luxor has a hot desert climate. The best months are October to April, when temperatures are mild (20–30°C / 68–86°F). Summer (May to September) is extremely hot, often exceeding 40°C (104°F), but prices drop by 30–50%.

Visa & Documents

Most nationalities can obtain a visa on arrival at Luxor Airport. The cost is $25 USD (cash only, exact change recommended). You will receive a sticker valid for 30 days. Alternatively, apply online for an e-Visa ($25+ $5 processing fee) before travel. Your passport must be valid for at least 6 months from the date of entry. No vaccinations are required, but Hepatitis A and Typhoid shots are recommended. Bring a photocopy of your passport and visa.
