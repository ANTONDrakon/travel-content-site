15 Best Things to Do in Luxor 2026

Luxor, often called the world’s greatest open-air museum, is a city in southern Egypt that sits on the east bank of the Nile River. It is home to the ancient city of Thebes, the capital of pharaonic Egypt during its golden age. In 2026, Luxor continues to captivate travelers with its unparalleled concentration of temples, tombs, and monuments spanning over 4,000 years of history. From the Valley of the Kings to the Karnak Temple Complex, every corner of Luxor offers a direct connection to the past. The city’s dry climate—averaging 35°C (95°F) in summer and 22°C (72°F) in winter—makes it a year-round destination, though October to April is ideal for comfortable exploration. This guide details the 15 best attractions, practical itineraries, tours, free sights, and essential tips for an unforgettable visit in 2026.

15 Best Attractions in Luxor

1. Valley of the Kings

This UNESCO World Heritage site on the west bank of Luxor contains 63 tombs of pharaohs from the New Kingdom (1550–1069 BCE), including the legendary tomb of Tutankhamun (KV62). The tombs are carved deep into the limestone hills, decorated with intricate hieroglyphics and scenes from the Book of the Dead. In 2026, ongoing conservation efforts have improved lighting and ventilation, making visits more comfortable. The site spans over 20 square kilometers and is a must-see for understanding ancient Egyptian burial practices.

  • Ticket Price: 440 EGP (approx. $14 USD) for entry to three tombs (excluding Tutankhamun’s tomb, which costs 500 EGP extra).
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:00 PM (winter); 6:00 AM to 6:00 PM (summer).
  • How to Get There: Take a ferry from the east bank to the west bank (5 EGP), then a taxi or minibus (50–100 EGP) to the site.
  • Tip: Arrive at opening time to avoid crowds and heat; bring water and a flashlight for darker tombs.

2. Karnak Temple Complex

Karnak is the largest religious complex ever built, covering 200 acres with a massive hypostyle hall of 134 columns, some reaching 21 meters (69 feet) high. Dedicated to the Theban triad of Amun, Mut, and Khonsu, it was expanded over 2,000 years by pharaohs like Ramesses II and Hatshepsut. The Sacred Lake, obelisks, and the Avenue of Sphinxes (restored in 2021) are highlights. In 2026, night sound-and-light shows offer a dramatic perspective.

  • Ticket Price: 300 EGP (approx. $10 USD) for general entry; 200 EGP for the sound-and-light show.
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:30 PM (winter); 6:00 AM to 6:30 PM (summer).
  • How to Get There: Located in central Luxor on the east bank; a 10-minute walk from Luxor Temple or a 20 EGP taxi ride from most hotels.
  • Tip: Hire a guide (200–300 EGP) to understand the complex layout and history.

3. Luxor Temple

This elegant temple, built primarily by Amenhotep III and Ramesses II, is located in the heart of modern Luxor on the east bank. It is dedicated to the rejuvenation of kingship and features a grand pylon, a colonnade of 14 papyrus-shaped columns, and statues of Ramesses II. The temple is particularly stunning at sunset when it is illuminated. In 2026, the adjacent Avenue of Sphinxes connects it directly to Karnak over a 2.7-kilometer (1.7-mile) route.

  • Ticket Price: 260 EGP (approx. $8.50 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 10:00 PM (last entry 9:00 PM).
  • How to Get There: Walk from most east bank hotels; it is on Sharia al-Mahata, near the Nile promenade.
  • Tip: Visit after 5:00 PM for cooler temperatures and fewer crowds.

4. Valley of the Queens

Located near the Valley of the Kings, this site contains over 90 tombs of queens and royal children from the 19th and 20th dynasties. The most famous is the tomb of Nefertari (QV66), wife of Ramesses II, which features vibrant wall paintings that have been meticulously restored. In 2026, the tomb of Nefertari remains one of the most expensive but rewarding attractions in Egypt. The site is less crowded than the Valley of the Kings, offering a more serene experience.

  • Ticket Price: 200 EGP (approx. $6.50 USD) for general entry; 1,400 EGP (approx. $45 USD) for Nefertari’s tomb.
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: A 10-minute taxi ride from the Valley of the Kings (50 EGP).
  • Tip: Book Nefertari’s tomb in advance during peak season (December–February).

5. Temple of Hatshepsut at Deir el-Bahri

This mortuary temple of Egypt’s most famous female pharaoh is carved into a sheer cliff face on the west bank. Its three terraces, colonnades, and reliefs depicting Hatshepsut’s expedition to Punt make it a masterpiece of ancient architecture. The temple is part of the Deir el-Bahri complex, which also includes tombs of nobles. In 2026, a new visitor center provides shade and exhibits.

  • Ticket Price: 240 EGP (approx. $8 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: A 20-minute taxi ride from the west bank ferry dock (80–100 EGP).
  • Tip: Combine with the Valley of the Kings and Valley of the Queens for a full west bank day.

6. Colossi of Memnon

These two massive stone statues of Pharaoh Amenhotep III, each 18 meters (59 feet) tall, guard the entrance to his mortuary temple (now largely destroyed). They are made of quartzite sandstone and weigh approximately 720 tons each. The statues are a free-to-view landmark on the west bank, visible from the road. In 2026, restoration of the surrounding area has created a small park with benches.

  • Ticket Price: Free (public access).
  • Hours: Always accessible (outdoor site).
  • How to Get There: Located on the road between the west bank ferry and the Valley of the Kings; a 10-minute taxi ride from the ferry.
  • Tip: Stop for 15 minutes on your way to other west bank sites; best photographed in early morning light.

7. Medinet Habu

This mortuary temple of Ramesses III is one of the best-preserved temples in Egypt, with massive walls, reliefs of battles, and a royal palace. It covers 7.5 hectares (18.5 acres) and is surrounded by a mudbrick enclosure. The temple’s vivid carvings depict the pharaoh’s victories over the Sea Peoples. In 2026, it remains less visited than Karnak, offering a more intimate experience.

  • Ticket Price: 140 EGP (approx. $4.50 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: A 15-minute taxi ride from the west bank ferry (60 EGP).
  • Tip: Pair with the Valley of the Queens, as they are close together.

8. Luxor Museum

This modern museum on the east bank houses a curated collection of artifacts from Luxor’s temples and tombs, including items from Tutankhamun’s tomb, mummies, and statues. Unlike the Egyptian Museum in Cairo, it is well-lit and organized by theme. In 2026, a new wing displays the “Golden Age of Thebes” with 3D reconstructions.

  • Ticket Price: 200 EGP (approx. $6.50 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 9:00 AM to 2:00 PM and 5:00 PM to 9:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: On the Corniche el-Nil, a 15-minute walk from Luxor Temple.
  • Tip: Visit in the evening after temple tours; photography is allowed without flash.

9. Mummification Museum

This small but fascinating museum focuses on the art and science of mummification, featuring mummies of animals (cats, crocodiles) and humans, along with tools and canopic jars. It is located on the east bank near the Luxor Temple. The exhibits explain the 70-day mummification process in detail. In 2026, new interactive displays allow visitors to virtually unwrap a mummy.

  • Ticket Price: 120 EGP (approx. $4 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 9:00 AM to 2:00 PM and 5:00 PM to 9:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: A 5-minute walk from Luxor Temple along the Corniche.
  • Tip: Combine with Luxor Museum for a half-day east bank cultural tour.

10. Ramesseum

This mortuary temple of Ramesses II features a fallen colossal statue of the pharaoh (inspiration for Shelley’s poem “Ozymandias”) and a large hypostyle hall. The temple covers 4 hectares (10 acres) and includes reliefs of the Battle of Kadesh. In 2026, ongoing excavations have uncovered new storage rooms. It is quieter than other west bank sites.

  • Ticket Price: 120 EGP (approx. $4 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: A 10-minute taxi ride from the Valley of the Kings (50 EGP).
  • Tip: Visit in the late afternoon for golden light on the ruins.

11. Tomb of Tutankhamun (KV62)

This small but iconic tomb in the Valley of the Kings contains the pharaoh’s mummy and original sarcophagus (the treasures are in the Egyptian Museum in Cairo). Discovered by Howard Carter in 1922, it has four chambers with faint wall paintings. In 2026, a replica tomb nearby offers a less crowded alternative, but the original remains a top attraction.

  • Ticket Price: 500 EGP (approx. $16 USD) on top of Valley of the Kings entry.
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: Inside the Valley of the Kings; follow signs from the main entrance.
  • Tip: Visit early to avoid long queues; the tomb is small and gets crowded quickly.

12. Tomb of Seti I (KV17)

This is the longest and most decorated tomb in the Valley of the Kings, stretching 137 meters (449 feet) with stunning reliefs in raised relief. It was closed for restoration for many years but reopened in 2023 with limited access. In 2026, it remains a premium attraction due to its exquisite art and size. The tomb costs extra to enter.

  • Ticket Price: 1,000 EGP (approx. $32 USD) on top of Valley of the Kings entry.
  • Hours: Daily 6:00 AM to 5:00 PM (limited capacity; book in advance).
  • How to Get There: Inside the Valley of the Kings; a 10-minute walk from the entrance.
  • Tip: This is a splurge; only visit if you are a serious Egyptology enthusiast.

13. Temple of Dendera

Located 60 kilometers (37 miles) north of Luxor, this well-preserved Ptolemaic temple is dedicated to the goddess Hathor. Its hypostyle hall, zodiac ceiling (the original is in the Louvre), and crypts are highlights. The temple complex includes a Roman-era mammisi (birth house) and a sacred lake. In 2026, a new road makes it easier to reach by taxi.

  • Ticket Price: 200 EGP (approx. $6.50 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 7:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: Taxi from Luxor (round trip 400–500 EGP) or join a half-day tour.
  • Tip: Combine with the Temple of Abydos (further north) for a full-day excursion.

14. Temple of Esna

This temple, dedicated to the god Khnum, is located 55 kilometers (34 miles) south of Luxor in the city of Esna. It is famous for its massive hypostyle hall with 24 columns and a well-preserved astronomical ceiling. The temple is 9 meters (30 feet) below street level due to centuries of Nile silt. In 2026, a new entrance platform improves accessibility.

  • Ticket Price: 120 EGP (approx. $4 USD).
  • Hours: Daily 7:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  • How to Get There: Taxi from Luxor (round trip 300–400 EGP) or by train from Luxor station (20 EGP).
  • Tip: Visit on the way to or from the Aswan region for convenience.

15. Hot Air Balloon Over Luxor

This is not a historical site but a unique experience offering aerial views of the west bank’s temples, tombs, and the Nile Valley. Flights typically launch at dawn from the west bank, lasting 30–45 minutes. In 2026, new safety regulations have made ballooning more reliable, with over 20 operators available. The view of the Valley of the Kings from above is unforgettable.

  • Ticket Price: 1,500–2,500 EGP (approx. $48–80 USD) depending on operator and season.
  • Hours: Daily at sunrise (around 5:30 AM in winter, 4:30 AM in summer).
  • How to Get There: Operators pick you up from your hotel; include transport to launch sites.
  • Tip: Book through a reputable company (e.g., Magic Horizon or Sindbad) and confirm insurance.

Optimal Itineraries for Luxor

1-Day Itinerary (East Bank Focus)

Start at 7:00 AM with a visit to Karnak Temple Complex (2.5 hours). Walk the Avenue of Sphinxes to Luxor Temple (1.5 hours). Lunch at a local restaurant (e.g., Sofra, 150 EGP). Afternoon: Visit Luxor Museum (1.5 hours) and the Mummification Museum (1 hour). Evening: Stroll the Corniche el-Nil and have dinner at a Nile-view restaurant. Total walking distance: 5 km (3 miles).

2-Day Itinerary (East and West Banks)

Day 1: Same as 1-day itinerary, but add a sunset visit to Luxor Temple (6:00 PM). Day 2: Start at 6:00 AM with a hot air balloon ride (breakfast included). Then, visit Valley of the Kings (3 tombs, 2 hours), Temple of Hatshepsut (1.5 hours), and Colossi of Memnon (15 minutes). Lunch at a west bank cafe (e.g., Marsam, 100 EGP). Afternoon: Medinet Habu (1.5 hours) and Ramesseum (1 hour). Return to east bank by ferry by 5:00 PM.

3-Day Itinerary (Full Exploration)

Day 1: East bank: Karnak, Luxor Temple, museums. Day 2: West bank: Valley of the Kings, Valley of the Queens
