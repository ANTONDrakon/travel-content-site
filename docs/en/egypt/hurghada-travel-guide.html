Hurghada Travel Guide 2026

Hurghada, once a sleepy fishing village, has transformed into Egypt’s premier Red Sea resort city, stretching over 40 kilometers of coastline. The city blends a vibrant tourist promenade, known as the Sheraton Road and Sakkala districts, with the laid-back charm of desert-meets-sea living. By 2026, Hurghada continues to attract over 3 million visitors annually, drawn by year-round sunshine, world-class diving, and a unique atmosphere where Bedouin culture meets European-style resort luxury. The air is dry, the water is crystal-clear, and the pace of life slows to the rhythm of the tide.

Why visit Hurghada in 2026? The city offers an unmatched combination of affordability and adventure. A week here costs roughly half of what you would spend in comparable Mediterranean resorts, yet you gain access to some of the planet’s best coral reefs, including the famous Giftun Islands and Abu Ramada. Beyond the beach, you can explore the Eastern Desert, visit monasteries dating back to the 4th century, or take a day trip to Luxor’s Valley of the Kings. The atmosphere is relaxed and welcoming, with a strong German, Russian, and British expat community ensuring that English and German are widely spoken alongside Arabic.

Hurghada is also a gateway to the Red Sea’s marine biodiversity. The water temperature averages 24°C (75°F) in winter and 28°C (82°F) in summer, making it a year-round diving paradise. The city has invested heavily in infrastructure for 2026, including a new marina, expanded airport terminals, and eco-friendly resorts. Whether you are a solo backpacker, a family with children, or a luxury traveler, Hurghada delivers an authentic Egyptian experience without the crowds of Cairo or the chaos of Sharm el-Sheikh.

How to Get There

Hurghada International Airport (HRG) is the main gateway, located about 10 kilometers south of the city center. By 2026, the airport handles over 50 airlines, including budget carriers like easyJet, Ryanair, and Wizz Air, as well as full-service airlines such as EgyptAir, Lufthansa, and Turkish Airlines. Direct flights from London Gatwick take approximately 4 hours 30 minutes, with average round-trip prices ranging from £180 to £350 depending on the season. From Berlin, flights cost around €150 to €300 round-trip, while from Moscow, prices start at $250 USD. For travelers from the United States, connecting flights via Istanbul or Cairo typically cost $500 to $800 round-trip. Charter flights from Scandinavian countries and Eastern Europe are also common, often bundled with package holidays.

Upon arrival, you can purchase a visa on arrival at the airport for $25 USD (see Visa section). Taxis from the airport to the city center cost a fixed 150 EGP (Egyptian pounds) for standard cars, or about $5 USD. Many hotels offer free airport transfers if you book directly.

Where to Stay

Hurghada is divided into three main areas: Downtown (including Sheraton Road and Sakkala), the resorts of El Gouna (25 km north), and the quieter southern coast near Makadi Bay and Sahl Hasheesh. Each offers a different experience.

Downtown Hurghada (Budget & Mid-Range)

Sheraton Road is the bustling heart, with cheap hotels, restaurants, and souvenir shops. Budget options like Sea Gull Hotel start at $15 per night for a double room. Mid-range hotels such as Golden Beach Hotel cost $30-50 per night and include breakfast. For a bit more comfort, Hilton Hurghada Plaza offers rooms from $70 per night.

El Gouna (Luxury & Water Sports)

El Gouna is a private, resort-style town 25 km north, designed around lagoons and golf courses. It is more expensive but offers higher quality. Movenpick Resort & Spa El Gouna starts at $120 per night, while the all-inclusive Steigenberger Resort costs $150-200 per night. Budget travelers can stay at Fanadir Residence for $40-60 per night.

Makadi Bay & Sahl Hasheesh (All-Inclusive)

These areas are south of the city, known for massive all-inclusive resorts. Jaz Makadi Star & Spa offers rooms from $80 per night including meals and drinks. Baron Palace Sahl Hasheesh is a top-end option at $200 per night. These resorts are isolated, so you will need taxis to explore the city.

Top 15 Attractions

  1. Giftun Islands National Park – A protected marine reserve with pristine beaches and coral reefs. Snorkel among clownfish and rays. Boat trips from Hurghada cost $20-40 per person. Open daily 8:00 AM to sunset. Park entry fee: $5 per person.
  2. Abu Ramada Reef – One of the best dive sites in the Red Sea, famous for dolphin sightings. Accessible by boat from Hurghada Marina. Dive trips cost $50-80 including equipment. No entry fee, but boat trips are required.
  3. Hurghada Grand Aquarium – A modern aquarium with a 24-meter tunnel, featuring sharks, rays, and sea turtles. Ticket price: $15 for adults, $8 for children. Open daily 9:00 AM to 10:00 PM.
  4. El Dahar Square (Old Town) – The historic center with a traditional souk, coffee shops, and the El Mina Mosque. Free entry. Open 24 hours, but shops close around 10:00 PM. Bargaining is expected.
  5. Hurghada Marina – A modern waterfront with restaurants, bars, and boat tours. Free to walk. Boat tours for sunset cost $10-20 per person. Open 24 hours.
  6. Sand City Hurghada – An outdoor museum with giant sand sculptures of pharaohs, animals, and movie characters. Ticket price: $10 for adults, $5 for children. Open 9:00 AM to 6:00 PM.
  7. Desert Safari & Bedouin Dinner – A 4-hour trip into the Eastern Desert, including quad biking, camel riding, and a traditional dinner. Costs $25-40 per person. Tours depart at 3:00 PM daily.
  8. El Gouna Golf Club – An 18-hole championship golf course designed by Karl Litten. Green fees: $80 for 18 holes, club rental $30. Open 7:00 AM to 7:00 PM.
  9. Mons Porphyrites – An ancient Roman quarry in the desert, 40 km from Hurghada. Free entry, but you need a 4x4 taxi ($50 round-trip). Best visited with a guide. Open daylight hours.
  10. Hurghada Museum – A modern museum showcasing Egyptian artifacts, including mummies and jewelry. Ticket price: $10 for adults, $5 for students. Open 9:00 AM to 5:00 PM (closed Fridays).
  11. Mahmya Island – A private island with white sand and eco-friendly facilities. Day trips cost $40-60 including lunch and snorkeling. Open 8:00 AM to 5:00 PM.
  12. Scuba Diving at the SS Thistlegorm – A world-famous WWII shipwreck, accessible by liveaboard or day boat from Hurghada. Dive trips cost $100-150. Requires advanced certification. Open year-round, but best conditions from March to November.
  13. Red Sea Waterworld – A water park with slides, wave pools, and lazy rivers. Ticket price: $20 for adults, $12 for children. Open 10:00 AM to 6:00 PM.
  14. Dolphin House Reef – A shallow reef near the coast where wild dolphins frequently swim. Free to snorkel from the beach, but boat tours cost $15-25. Open 24 hours.
  15. Al-Qusair – A historic port town 130 km south, with Ottoman fortresses and coral reefs. Day trip by bus costs $10 round-trip. Free entry to the fort. Open daylight hours.

Getting Around

Hurghada has no metro or rail system. The main modes of transport are taxis, microbuses, and rental cars. Taxis are plentiful and cheap: a ride within the city costs 20-50 EGP ($0.70-1.70). Always agree on the price before getting in. Uber operates in Hurghada, with fares starting at 30 EGP ($1) for short trips. Microbuses follow fixed routes and cost 5 EGP ($0.17) per ride, but they are crowded and confusing for tourists. Rental cars are available from Avis, Hertz, and local agencies, starting at $25 per day for a compact car. Gasoline costs about 10 EGP ($0.34) per liter. Driving is chaotic but manageable if you stay alert. For airport transfers, many hotels offer shuttles for $10-20 one way.

Where to Eat

Hurghada’s cuisine is a mix of Egyptian staples, fresh seafood, and international fare. Street food is cheap and delicious. Try koshari (rice, lentils, pasta, and tomato sauce) for 10-20 EGP ($0.35-0.70). Ful medames (fava beans) and ta’amiya (Egyptian falafel) are also common, costing 5-10 EGP. For seafood, head to Star Fish Restaurant on Sheraton Road, where a grilled fish meal costs 150-250 EGP ($5-9). Mid-range restaurants like El Dar Darak serve Egyptian dishes for 100-200 EGP ($3.50-7). Fine dining options include La Rotonde in El Gouna, where a three-course meal costs 400-600 EGP ($14-21). Street food is safest at busy stalls with high turnover. Always drink bottled water.

Best Time to Visit

Hurghada has a subtropical desert climate with hot summers and mild winters. The best time to visit is from October to April, when temperatures range from 20°C to 30°C (68-86°F). December and January are the coolest months, with highs of 22°C (72°F) and lows of 12°C (54°F). This is the peak season for European tourists, so prices for accommodation and flights are 20-30% higher. May through September are very hot, with July and August averaging 38°C (100°F) and occasionally hitting 45°C (113°F). However, this is the low season, and you can find hotel rooms for 40% less. The Red Sea water is warmest in August and September at 29°C (84°F), ideal for snorkeling. March and November offer a good balance of pleasant weather and lower prices. Avoid June if you dislike high humidity, which is rare but possible.

Visa & Documents

Citizens of most countries, including the United States, Canada, the United Kingdom, Australia, and the European Union, can obtain a visa on arrival at Hurghada Airport. The cost is $25 USD (cash only, in exact change). You will fill out a small form and present your passport with at least 6 months validity. The visa is a sticker placed in your passport, valid for 30 days. Alternatively, you can apply for an e-visa online before travel through the official Egyptian visa portal, costing $25 plus a processing fee. This is recommended to avoid queues. Citizens of some African and Asian countries may need a visa in advance. Check with the Egyptian embassy. You must also have a return ticket and proof of accommodation. Travel insurance is strongly recommended.

10 Travel Tips

  1. Bargain respectfully. Prices in souks and for taxis are negotiable. Start at 50% of the asking price and work up. Do not get angry; it is part of the culture.
  2. Carry cash. Many small shops, taxis, and street vendors do not accept credit cards. ATMs are widely available in the city center but may charge fees. Egyptian pounds (EGP) are preferred, but USD and EUR are accepted at some hotels.
