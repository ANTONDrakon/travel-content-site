Sharm El Sheikh Travel Guide 2026

Sharm El Sheikh is a vibrant resort city perched on the southern tip of the Sinai Peninsula, where the crystal-clear waters of the Red Sea meet the stark, dramatic peaks of the Sinai mountains. Once a quiet fishing village, it has transformed into Egypt’s premier beach destination, drawing over 5 million visitors annually before the pandemic, with numbers rebounding strongly to over 3 million in 2025. The city is defined by its unique atmosphere—a fusion of world-class diving, laid-back beach culture, and a bustling nightlife scene centered around Naama Bay and the newer Soho Square. The air is dry and warm, the water is a constant 24-28°C, and the desert backdrop provides a stunning, almost lunar landscape that feels a world away from the chaos of Cairo.

Why visit in 2026? The answer is simple: accessibility, affordability, and unparalleled underwater beauty. Sharm El Sheikh offers some of the best snorkeling and diving on the planet, with sites like Ras Mohammed National Park and the Thistlegorm wreck attracting enthusiasts from every continent. For non-divers, the city boasts luxurious all-inclusive resorts, world-class spas, and family-friendly water parks like Aqua Blue. Recent infrastructure investments, including a modernized airport and improved road networks, have made travel smoother than ever. Moreover, Egypt’s currency devaluation has made the city exceptionally budget-friendly for international tourists, with prices often 30-50% lower than comparable destinations in the Caribbean or Southeast Asia.

The city is also a gateway to adventure. Beyond the beaches, you can take a quad bike safari into the Colored Canyon, ride a camel at sunset, or visit St. Catherine’s Monastery—a UNESCO World Heritage site at the foot of Mount Sinai. Sharm El Sheikh is not just a resort; it’s a launchpad for exploring the ancient and natural wonders of the Sinai. Whether you’re a solo traveler seeking solitude, a couple on a romantic getaway, or a family looking for a safe, sun-drenched holiday, Sharm delivers with a consistency that keeps visitors returning year after year.

How to Get There

Sharm El Sheikh International Airport (SSH) is your main entry point. It receives direct flights from over 30 cities across Europe, the Middle East, and Russia. In 2026, expect expanded routes from low-cost carriers like Wizz Air, Ryanair, and easyJet, alongside charter flights from tour operators. From the United Kingdom, direct flights from London Gatwick, Manchester, and Birmingham take approximately 5 hours, with return fares averaging $180-$350 in low season and $350-$600 in peak summer. From Germany (Frankfurt, Berlin), prices range from $200-$400. From Italy (Rome, Milan), expect $150-$350. From Russia (Moscow, St. Petersburg), direct flights are frequent, averaging $250-$450. Note that flights from the United States typically require a connection in Europe (e.g., Istanbul, London, Frankfurt), with total travel time 12-18 hours and prices from $600-$1,200.

For those already in Egypt, you can fly from Cairo (1 hour, $60-$120 one-way) or take a bus via the 12-hour journey through the Sinai desert (prices $15-$30). The airport is located about 10 km from Naama Bay, and a taxi to your hotel should cost $10-$20.

Where to Stay

Sharm El Sheikh is divided into distinct neighborhoods, each offering a different experience. Naama Bay is the historic heart—bustling, with restaurants, bars, and shops. It’s ideal for nightlife lovers and first-time visitors. Hotels here range from budget-friendly to mid-range.

Hadaba and Nabq Bay are quieter, more family-oriented areas with long stretches of beach and all-inclusive resorts. Shark’s Bay is famous for its diving centers and quieter atmosphere. For luxury, head to the Four Seasons Resort (from $400/night) or the Ritz-Carlton (from $350/night). For mid-range, the Mercure Naama Bay (from $80/night) or the Hilton Sharks Bay (from $100/night) offer great value. Budget travelers can find hostels and guesthouses in the Old Market area, like the Camel Dive Camp (from $25/night for a dorm).

Top 15 Attractions

  1. Ras Mohammed National Park — Egypt’s premier dive and snorkel site, with stunning coral reefs and the famous "Magic Lake." Entry: $10 per person. Open daily 8:00 AM–5:00 PM.
  2. Naama Bay — The main tourist hub with a long promenade, shops, and beach clubs. Free to walk. Open 24/7.
  3. Thistlegorm Wreck — A world-famous WWII shipwreck dive site, accessible only by boat. Dive trip cost: $80-$120 including gear. Dives typically 8:00 AM–3:00 PM.
  4. St. Catherine’s Monastery — A 6th-century monastery at the foot of Mount Sinai. Entry: $15. Open 9:00 AM–12:00 PM (closed Fridays and Sundays).
  5. Mount Sinai — Climb the 2,285-meter peak for sunrise. Guided tours from $40. Start at midnight to reach the top by 5:00 AM.
  6. Colored Canyon — A striking geological formation with red, yellow, and purple rock walls. Entry: $10. Open 8:00 AM–4:00 PM.
  7. Soho Square — A modern entertainment complex with ice skating, a bowling alley, and restaurants. Free entry; activities from $10. Open 10:00 AM–2:00 AM.
  8. Old Market — A traditional bazaar with spices, perfumes, and souvenirs. Free entry. Open 9:00 AM–11:00 PM.
  9. Sharm El Sheikh Museum — Showcasing Sinai’s history and artifacts. Entry: $8. Open 9:00 AM–5:00 PM (closed Mondays).
  10. Aqua Blue Water Park — A large water park with 20 slides and pools. Entry: $30 adults, $20 children. Open 10:00 AM–6:00 PM.
  11. Ras Um Sid — A popular snorkeling spot with easy shore access. Free. Open 24/7.
  12. Al Mustafa Mosque — A beautiful modern mosque with intricate architecture. Free. Open 9:00 AM–8:00 PM (non-prayer times).
  13. Nabq Bay Nature Reserve — A protected area with mangroves, desert, and beaches. Entry: $5. Open 8:00 AM–4:00 PM.
  14. Shark’s Bay Beach — A calm, shallow bay perfect for families. Free entry; loungers from $5. Open 24/7.
  15. Desert Safari (Quad Biking) — A thrilling ride through the Sinai desert. Tours from $30. Typically 2-3 hours, starting at 4:00 PM for sunset.

Getting Around

Sharm El Sheikh does not have a metro or public bus system. The primary modes of transport are taxis, ride-hailing apps, and rental cars. Taxis are plentiful but negotiate the price before getting in. A short ride within Naama Bay costs $3-$5, while a trip from Naama to Nabq Bay is $10-$15. Uber and Careem operate in the city, offering fixed rates (e.g., Naama to airport: $8-$12).

For independence, rent a car from $25/day (manual) or $35/day (automatic) from agencies like Budget or local operators. Fuel is cheap at $0.30 per liter. Alternatively, rent a scooter for $15/day. Buses are limited to tourist shuttles between hotels and the airport, costing $5-$10 per trip. Most visitors rely on taxis or organized tours for longer distances.

Where to Eat

Sharm El Sheikh offers a diverse culinary scene, from Egyptian street food to international fine dining. Local staples include koshari (rice, lentils, pasta, and tomato sauce), ful medames (stewed fava beans), and fresh seafood grilled with lemon and garlic. Street food is cheap: a falafel sandwich costs $0.50, and a shawarma wrap is $1-$2.

For sit-down meals, head to Naama Bay. Fares Seafood Restaurant offers a mixed grill for $15. El Masrien serves authentic Egyptian cuisine, with a full meal for $10. For fine dining, Il Frantoio (Italian) at the Four Seasons costs $50-$80 per person. Soho Square has international options like Hard Rock Cafe (burgers from $15) and La Veranda (French, $40-$60). Budget travelers can eat well at local cafes for $5-$10 per meal. Tipping 10-15% is customary.

Best Time to Visit

Sharm El Sheikh enjoys a subtropical desert climate with over 350 days of sunshine per year. The best time to visit is from March to June and September to November, when temperatures are pleasant (25-35°C) and crowds are smaller. July and August are peak season, with temperatures soaring to 40-45°C, but the sea breeze makes it bearable. Prices for flights and hotels rise 30-50% during this period. December to February is the coolest, with daytime highs of 20-25°C and chilly nights (10-15°C), making it ideal for sightseeing and hiking. This is also the best time for budget travelers, as rates drop significantly.

Visa & Documents

Most nationalities, including US, UK, EU, Canadian, and Australian citizens, can obtain a visa on arrival at Sharm El Sheikh Airport. The cost is $25 USD (cash only, exact change preferred). The visa is a single-entry sticker valid for 30 days. Alternatively, you can apply for an e-visa online at the official Egyptian visa portal ($25 plus processing fee) before traveling. Your passport must be valid for at least six months from your date of entry. For stays limited to the Sharm El Sheikh resort area (including Ras Mohammed and Mount Sinai), a free 15-day "Sinai-only" stamp is available at the airport—ask the immigration officer. This does not allow travel to Cairo or Luxor.

10 Travel Tips

  1. Bring cash in USD or EUR. ATMs are common in Naama Bay but may charge high fees. Many vendors prefer cash, especially for small purchases. Egyptian pounds (EGP) are used locally; exchange rates are favorable at banks or exchange offices.
  2. Dress modestly outside resorts. While swimwear is fine on the beach, cover shoulders and knees when visiting the Old Market, mosques, or local neighborhoods. This shows respect and avoids unwanted attention.
  3. Bargain politely. Haggling is expected in markets and with taxi drivers. Start at 50% of the quoted price and negotiate to a fair middle ground. A smile goes a long way.
  4. Book dives and tours in advance. Popular sites like Ras Mohammed and the Thistlegorm can sell out in peak season. Use reputable operators like Emperor Divers or Camel Dive Club. Prices are 20-30% cheaper if booked online before arrival.
  5. Stay hydrated but avoid tap water. Drink only bottled or filtered water. Dehydration is common in the heat. Carry a reusable bottle and refill at hotel water stations (usually free).
  6. Respect local customs during Ramadan. If visiting during the holy month (expected March 1-29, 2026), avoid eating, drinking, or smoking in public during daylight hours. Many restaurants are closed until sunset, but hotels serve meals discreetly.
  7. Use sunscreen and reef-safe products. The sun is intense year-round. SPF 50+ is essential. Avoid sunscreens with oxybenzone, which damages coral reefs—many shops sell reef-safe brands.
  8. Learn a few Arabic phrases. "Shukran" (thank you), "Salam Alaikum" (hello), and "Kam?" (how much?) are appreciated. English is widely spoken in tourist areas, but locals warm to efforts.
  9. Be cautious with desert tours. Choose operators with good reviews (e.g., on TripAdvisor). Avoid extremely cheap tours ($15 or less) as they may cut corners on safety. Always carry water and a scarf for dust.
  10. Check your flight’s visa requirements. If you have a connecting flight in a country like Turkey or Saudi Arabia, ensure you meet transit visa rules. For the Sinai-only stamp, you cannot leave the Sharm region—plan accordingly.
