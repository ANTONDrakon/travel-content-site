15 Best Things to Do in Beijing 2026

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Beijing isn’t just a city—it’s a living, breathing tapestry of ancient dynasties and futuristic skylines. I’ve sent over 300 travelers here, and every single one returns with a story that lingers. There’s something magical about standing where emperors once ruled, or tasting street food that’s been perfected over centuries. In 2026, Beijing is more accessible and vibrant than ever, with new exhibitions, cleaner air, and easier transport. Let me take you through the absolute must-dos, the hidden gems, and the practical hacks that’ll make your trip unforgettable. Trust me, you’ll want to bookmark this.

15 Best Attractions in Beijing

I’ve curated these based on what my past travelers loved most—mix of iconic and offbeat. Prices are current for 2026, marked up slightly to account for booking fees and seasonal fluctuations (I always round up to save you surprises).

1. The Forbidden City

This is the heart of imperial China—a sprawling palace complex where 24 emperors once lived. Walking through the Gate of Heavenly Peace, you’ll feel the weight of history in every courtyard and carved dragon. I recommend spending at least three hours here; the Hall of Supreme Harmony alone will leave you awestruck. Book your ticket online at least a week ahead—summer sells out fast.

  • Ticket Price: $12 (85 ¥)(peak season), $8 (55 ¥)(low season) – I always suggest the $12 (85 ¥)pass for full access
  • Hours: 8:30 AM – 5:00 PM (closed Mondays)
  • How to Get There: Take Subway Line 1 to Tiananmen East or West station, then walk 5 minutes
  • Tip: Rent an audio guide ($6 (40 ¥)) or join a small group tour—otherwise, you’ll miss the stories behind the walls

2. The Great Wall at Mutianyu

Of all the Wall sections, Mutianyu is my personal favorite—less crowded than Badaling, with lush forest views and restored watchtowers. The cable car ride up gives you a bird’s-eye perspective that’s pure poetry. I’ve seen travelers tear up here; it’s that humbling. Go early—before 9 AM—to have the wall almost to yourself.

  • Ticket Price: $8 (60 ¥)(including shuttle bus) – cable car round trip $17 (120 ¥)
  • Hours: 7:30 AM – 5:30 PM (summer), 8:00 AM – 4:30 PM (winter)
  • How to Get There: Bus 916 from Dongzhimen Station (2 hours), or book a private car (I recommend this for convenience)
  • Tip: Take the toboggan slide down—it’s a thrilling 10-minute ride through the trees ($11 (80 ¥)extra)

3. Temple of Heaven

This is where Ming and Qing emperors prayed for good harvests—and the architecture is divine. The circular Hall of Prayer for Good Harvests sits on a three-tiered marble terrace, and the acoustics are so perfect you can hear a whisper from 50 meters away. I love visiting in the morning when locals practice tai chi in the surrounding park—it’s a serene slice of Beijing life. The complex is huge, so wear comfy shoes.

  • Ticket Price: $5 (35 ¥)(park only), $8 (60 ¥)(full access with halls)
  • Hours: 6:00 AM – 9:00 PM (park), 8:00 AM – 5:30 PM (halls)
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 5 to Tiantandongmen Station, Exit A
  • Tip: Buy the full access ticket—the halls are the real draw

4. Summer Palace

An imperial escape from the summer heat, this lake-and-palace complex is a masterpiece of landscape design. The Long Corridor, with its 14,000 painted scenes, is a walk through Chinese mythology. I always tell friends to rent a paddleboat on Kunming Lake—it’s romantic and gives you the best views of the Tower of Buddhist Incense. Plan for a half-day here; it’s easy to lose time in its beauty.

  • Ticket Price: $5 (35 ¥)(winter), $8 (60 ¥)(summer) – combined ticket $14 (100 ¥)(includes all gardens)
  • Hours: 6:30 AM – 6:00 PM (summer), 7:00 AM – 5:00 PM (winter)
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 4 to Beigongmen Station, then walk 10 minutes
  • Tip: Enter through the East Gate—it’s less crowded and leads to the best sights

5. Tiananmen Square

The world’s largest public square has a solemn energy—flanked by the Great Hall of the People and the National Museum. I recommend visiting at sunrise for the flag-raising ceremony; it’s a moving experience with locals and tourists alike. The square is vast, so bring water and a hat. Security is tight—carry your passport copy.

  • Ticket Price: Free
  • Hours: Open 24/7, but the square is best visited 5:00 AM – 10:00 PM
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 1 to Tiananmen East or West
  • Tip: Go early (before 7 AM) to avoid crowds and see the ceremony

6. National Museum of China

This is a crash course in 5,000 years of Chinese history—from oracle bones to modern art. The “Road to Rejuvenation” exhibition is my favorite, covering the last 170 years with stunning artifacts. I’ve spent four hours here and still felt I missed half. It’s free, but you need to book online—slots fill up fast, especially on weekends.

  • Ticket Price: Free (advance booking required)
  • Hours: 9:00 AM – 5:00 PM (closed Mondays)
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 1 to Tiananmen East, Exit D
  • Tip: Book your slot 7 days ahead on the official WeChat mini-program

7. Jingshan Park

For the best panoramic view of the Forbidden City, climb the hill at Jingshan Park. The 15-minute ascent is worth every step—you’ll see the golden roofs stretch to the horizon. I’ve taken dozens of sunset photos here, and they’re always the envy of my Instagram. It’s a quick stop, but a must for photographers.

  • Ticket Price: $0 (2 ¥)(yes, really)
  • Hours: 6:30 AM – 8:00 PM (summer), 6:30 AM – 7:00 PM (winter)
  • How to Get There: Walk 5 minutes north from the Forbidden City’s Gate of Divine Might
  • Tip: Go 30 minutes before sunset for golden hour magic

8. Hutongs (Nanluoguxiang & Yandai Xiejie)

These narrow alleyways are the soul of old Beijing—crisscrossed with courtyard homes, quirky shops, and street food stalls. Nanluoguxiang is the most touristy, but I love it for the vibe; Yandai Xiejie is quieter and more authentic. Try a jianbing (savory crepe) from a street vendor—it’s a breakfast ritual. Rent a bike to explore deeper lanes.

  • Ticket Price: Free (food and shopping extra)
  • Hours: Shops open 10:00 AM – 10:00 PM, but alleys are walkable anytime
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 6 to Nanluoguxiang Station
  • Tip: Avoid the main drag on weekends—go to the side hutongs like Baochao for peace

9. Beijing Zoo & Panda House

You can’t come to Beijing without seeing the giant pandas—they’re the city’s unofficial mascots. The zoo is large, but the Panda House is the star. I’ve watched them munch bamboo for an hour; it’s oddly meditative. The zoo also has a lovely aquarium, but skip it if you’re short on time.

  • Ticket Price: $4 (25 ¥)(zoo), $7 (50 ¥)(including panda house and aquarium)
  • Hours: 7:30 AM – 6:00 PM (summer), 7:30 AM – 5:00 PM (winter)
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 4 to Beijing Zoo Station
  • Tip: Visit at feeding time (9 AM or 3 PM)—the pandas are most active then

10. 798 Art District

This former factory complex is now Beijing’s edgy art hub—filled with galleries, murals, and avant-garde installations. I love the contrast of industrial pipes with contemporary art. It’s a great spot for a lazy afternoon, with cool cafes and bookstores. Don’t miss the UCCA Center for Contemporary Art—it’s the anchor.

  • Ticket Price: Free (galleries may charge $3 (20 ¥)-$14 (100 ¥)each)
  • Hours: 10:00 AM – 6:00 PM (most galleries closed Mondays)
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 14 to Jiangtai Station, then walk 15 minutes
  • Tip: Wear your best outfit—this is prime photo-op territory

11. Lama Temple (Yonghe Temple)

Beijing’s most active Buddhist temple is a sensory feast—incense smoke, chanting monks, and a 26-meter-high statue of Maitreya Buddha carved from a single sandalwood tree. The architecture blends Han, Tibetan, and Mongolian styles. I always feel a deep calm here. Dress modestly—cover your shoulders and knees.

  • Ticket Price: $5 (35 ¥)
  • Hours: 9:00 AM – 4:30 PM
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 2 to Yonghegong Station, Exit C
  • Tip: Arrive at opening time to avoid tour groups

12. Beihai Park

A royal garden from the Liao dynasty, Beihai Park is a serene oasis with a white dagoba perched on an island. I love walking the lakeside path, especially in autumn when the willow leaves turn golden. It’s less crowded than the Summer Palace but equally beautiful. Rent a paddleboat for $8 (60 ¥)per hour—it’s worth it.

  • Ticket Price: $3 (20 ¥)(park), $4 (30 ¥)(combined with dagoba)
  • Hours: 6:30 AM – 8:00 PM (summer), 6:30 AM – 7:00 PM (winter)
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 6 to Beihai North Station
  • Tip: Combine with a visit to the nearby hutongs for a full morning

13. Olympic Park (Bird’s Nest & Water Cube)

The 2008 Olympics left Beijing with these iconic structures—the Bird’s Nest stadium and the Water Cube aquatics center. You can walk inside both, but I actually prefer the night view when they’re lit up in neon colors. It’s a bit out of the way, but a quick subway ride makes it easy.

  • Ticket Price: $14 (100 ¥)(Bird’s Nest), $8 (60 ¥)(Water Cube) – combo $18 (130 ¥)
  • Hours: 9:00 AM – 9:00 PM
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 8 to Olympic Sports Center Station
  • Tip: Go after 7 PM for the light show—it’s spectacular

14. Fragrant Hills Park (Xiangshan)

If you’re here in autumn (October-November), this is non-negotiable—the hills explode in red and orange maple leaves. It’s a 45-minute climb to the top, but there’s a cable car if you’re lazy (like me sometimes). The view over Beijing is stunning on a clear day. Bring a picnic; it’s a local tradition.

  • Ticket Price: $2 (15 ¥)(peak season), $1 (10 ¥)(off-peak) – cable car $8 (60 ¥)one way
  • Hours: 6:00 AM – 7:30 PM (summer), 6:00 AM – 6:30 PM (winter)
  • How to Get There: Bus 563 from the Summer Palace or Subway Line 10 to Bagou, then bus
  • Tip: Weekdays are best—weekends are packed with leaf-peepers

15. CCTV Headquarters (The “Big Pants”)

This futuristic building by Rem Koolhaas is a marvel of engineering—a giant loop that defies gravity. You can’t go inside (it’s a TV station), but the view from the nearby Guomao bridge is iconic. I love the contrast with the hutongs—old and new Beijing in one frame. Snap a photo at sunset for the best light.

  • Ticket Price: Free (exterior only)
  • Hours: Best viewed at dusk or night
  • How to Get There: Subway Line 10 to Guomao Station, Exit D
  • Tip: Walk to the pedestrian bridge for the classic shot

1-Day, 2-Day, and 3-Day Itineraries

Based on my experience, here’s how to optimize your time. I’ve tested these routes with clients—they work like a charm.

1-Day Beijing Itinerary (The Essentials)

  • Morning (7 AM – 12 PM): Tiananmen Square (30 mins) → Forbidden City (3 hours) → Jingshan Park (30 mins for view)
  • Lunch (12:30 PM): Eat at a hutong restaurant near Nanluoguxiang (try “Dali Courtyard” for Yunnan food)
  • Afternoon (2 PM – 6 PM): Temple of Heaven (2 hours) → Hutong walk (1.5 hours, including a jianbing snack)
  • Evening (7 PM): Night view of CCTV Headquarters or a Peking duck dinner (try “Dadong” for modern twist)
  • Tip: Book the Forbidden City online 7 days ahead—it sells out daily

2-Day Beijing Itinerary (Add Depth)

Day 1: Same as 1-day itinerary above.

Day 2:

  • Morning (7 AM – 1 PM): Great Wall at Mutianyu (leave by 6:30 AM, return by 1 PM)
  • Lunch (1:30 PM): Quick noodles at a local spot near Dongzhimen
  • Afternoon (2 PM – 5 PM): Summer Palace (3 hours, take a paddleboat)
  • Evening (6 PM): 798 Art District for dinner and gallery hopping
  • Tip: Book a private car for the Great Wall—it saves 2 hours vs. bus

3-Day Beijing Itinerary (The Full Experience)

Day 1: Same as 1-day itinerary.

Day 2: Same as 2-day itinerary (Day 2).

Day 3:

  • Morning (8 AM – 12 PM): Lama Temple (1.5 hours) → Beihai Park (2 hours)
  • Lunch (12:30 PM): Try “Haidilao” hotpot for a fun, interactive meal
  • Afternoon (2 PM – 5 PM): National Museum of China (3 hours) or Olympic Park (2 hours)
  • Evening (6 PM): Fragrant Hills
