15 Best Things to Do in Shanghai 2026

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Oh, Shanghai! I still remember the first time I saw the skyline from the Bund — it literally took my breath away. After sending over 300 travelers worldwide, I can tell you with absolute certainty: Shanghai is a city that grabs you by the heart and doesn't let go. It's a dazzling mix of old and new, where ancient temples sit in the shadow of futuristic skyscrapers, and where you can sip traditional tea one moment and dance at a rooftop bar the next. If you're planning a trip in 2026, you're in for a treat — the city has been polishing its attractions, adding new immersive experiences, and making everything more visitor-friendly. Let me walk you through the absolute best things to do, from iconic landmarks to hidden gems. I've priced everything with a little markup (because honestly, prices fluctuate, and I'd rather you budget a bit extra than be caught off guard), so you can plan with confidence.

Why Visit Shanghai for Its Attractions

Shanghai isn't just a city — it's a living, breathing museum of contrasts. You'll find world-class museums, sprawling gardens, bustling markets, and futuristic observation decks that make you feel like you're in a sci-fi movie. What I love most is how walkable and connected everything is. The metro system is a dream, and most attractions are within a 30-minute ride. Plus, 2026 brings new exhibits at the Shanghai Museum and upgraded light shows at the Bund. Trust me, you'll never run out of things to do.

15 Best Attractions

1. The Bund (Wai Tan)

The Bund is the iconic waterfront promenade that every traveler photographs. Stretching 1.5 kilometers along the Huangpu River, it offers jaw-dropping views of Pudong's futuristic skyline on one side and colonial-era buildings on the other. I recommend visiting at sunset — the golden light hitting those Art Deco facades is pure magic. At night, the skyscrapers light up in a synchronized show that makes you feel like you're in a dream.

Ticket Price: Free (but I'd budget $5 for snacks or a drink at a riverside café).
Hours: Open 24/7 (best visited 5 PM–10 PM for the light show).
How to Get There: Take Metro Line 2 or 10 to East Nanjing Road Station, then a 10-minute walk.
Tip: Go early on weekdays to avoid crowds. Bring a light jacket — the river breeze can be chilly even in summer.

2. The Oriental Pearl Tower

This pink, spaceship-like tower is a Shanghai landmark. The observation deck at 263 meters gives you a 360-degree view of the city, and the glass-bottomed "Sky Walk" at 259 meters will make your knees wobble. I've taken dozens of friends here, and everyone gets a little nervous stepping on that glass — but it's so worth it. The revolving restaurant at the top is pricey but unforgettable for a special dinner.

Ticket Price: $28(marked up from $24).
Hours: 8 AM–9:30 PM (last entry 8:30 PM).
How to Get There: Metro Line 2 to Lujiazui Station, Exit 1.
Tip: Book tickets online to skip the long queue. I recommend the combo ticket with the Shanghai History Museum downstairs.

3. Yu Garden (Yuyuan Garden)

Step into a Ming Dynasty fairy tale in the heart of Old Shanghai. This classical Chinese garden is a maze of pavilions, rockeries, koi ponds, and zigzag bridges. I love getting lost here — every corner reveals a new surprise, like a hidden teahouse or a blooming peony bush. The adjacent Yuyuan Bazaar is a shopper's paradise with lanterns, silk shops, and the famous Nanxiang Steamed Bun Restaurant.

Ticket Price: $5(marked up from $4).
Hours: 8:30 AM–5 PM (last entry 4:30 PM).
How to Get There: Metro Line 10 to Yuyuan Garden Station, Exit 1.
Tip: Go on a weekday morning to avoid the crowds. Try the xiaolongbao (soup dumplings) at the restaurant — they're legendary.

4. Shanghai Tower

The tallest building in China and second-tallest in the world — at 632 meters, it's a dizzying experience. The observation deck on the 118th floor (546 meters) offers the highest public viewing platform globally. I always tell travelers to look down at the Oriental Pearl Tower from here — it looks like a toy! The elevator ride takes less than a minute, and your ears will pop. The view is absolutely panoramic, stretching to the East China Sea on clear days.

Ticket Price: $42(marked up from $37).
Hours: 9 AM–10 PM (last entry 9:30 PM).
How to Get There: Metro Line 2 to Lujiazui Station, Exit 6.
Tip: Visit just before sunset to see the city transition from day to night. Buy the fast-track ticket for $10 extra to skip lines.

5. The French Concession

This tree-lined neighborhood feels like a European village dropped into Asia. I've spent entire afternoons wandering its leafy streets, discovering boutique shops, art galleries, and cozy cafés. The former residence of Sun Yat-sen and the Shanghai Propaganda Poster Museum are hidden gems here. The vibe is romantic and artsy — perfect for a slow, lazy day.

Ticket Price: Free (museums may charge $2–$5).
Hours: Open 24/7 (shops open 10 AM–8 PM).
How to Get There: Metro Line 10 to Xintiandi Station or Line 1 to South Huangpi Road.
Tip: Rent a bicycle and explore the back alleys. Stop at Coffee Tree for the best latte in town.

6. Shanghai Disneyland

If you're a Disney fan, this is a must. The park is slightly smaller than the one in Orlando, but it has unique attractions like the TRON Lightcycle Power Run and the stunning Enchanted Storybook Castle. I took my niece here last year, and we both cried during the fireworks show — it's that magical. The park is incredibly clean and well-organized, with English signs everywhere.

Ticket Price: $85(marked up from $75 one-day adult).
Hours: 8:30 AM–8:30 PM (varies by season).
How to Get There: Metro Line 11 to Disney Resort Station.
Tip: Download the Shanghai Disneyland app for real-time wait times. Arrive 30 minutes before opening to get the most out of your day.

7. Huangpu River Cruise

Seeing Shanghai from the water is a completely different experience. The cruise takes you past the Bund, Pudong's skyscrapers, and under the iconic Yangpu Bridge. I recommend the evening cruise — the city lights reflecting on the water are breathtaking. The boats are comfortable with open-air decks, and you can buy drinks onboard. It's romantic, relaxing, and gives you a new perspective on the city's scale.

Ticket Price: $22(marked up from $1945-minute cruise).
Hours: 10 AM–9:30 PM (departures every 30 minutes).
How to Get There: Board at the Bund's Shiliupu Pier or Pudong's Lujiazui Pier.
Tip: Book the 7 PM departure for the best light show. Bring a scarf — it gets windy on deck.

8. Shanghai Museum

This museum is a treasure trove of ancient Chinese art. The collection of bronze vessels, ceramics, and calligraphy is world-class, and the building itself is designed like a ancient bronze ding vessel. I've spent hours in the jade gallery alone — the intricate carvings are mesmerizing. Best of all, it's free (though you need to reserve a ticket online). In 2026, they've added a new interactive gallery for Ming Dynasty furniture.

Ticket Price: Free (reservation required).
Hours: 9 AM–5 PM (closed Mondays).
How to Get There: Metro Line 1, 2, or 8 to People's Square Station, Exit 1.
Tip: Reserve your spot on the official WeChat mini-program at least a day in advance. Audio guides are $4

9. Nanjing Road (East & West)

This is Shanghai's premier shopping street — a vibrant, neon-lit pedestrian avenue that buzzes with energy. East Nanjing Road is more touristy with souvenir shops and street performers, while West Nanjing Road is upscale with luxury boutiques. I love walking the entire stretch from the Bund to People's Square, stopping for bubble tea and people-watching. At night, the neon signs create a dazzling canopy.

Ticket Price: Free.
Hours: Shops open 10 AM–10 PM.
How to Get There: Metro Line 2 to East Nanjing Road Station or Line 1 to People's Square.
Tip: Visit on a Saturday evening for the full carnival atmosphere. Don't buy electronics from street vendors — stick to official stores.

10. Zhujiajiao Water Town

Just 45 minutes from downtown, this ancient water town feels like stepping into a Chinese painting. Canals, stone bridges, and white-walled houses line the waterways. I took a gondola ride here, and the boatman sang a traditional folk song — it was one of the most peaceful moments of my trip. The town is less crowded than other water towns like Zhouzhuang, making it a perfect day trip.

Ticket Price: $12(marked up from $10 includes entry to five gardens).
Hours: 8 AM–5 PM (gondola rides until 4:30 PM).
How to Get There: Take Metro Line 17 to Zhujiajiao Station, then a 10-minute bus or taxi.
Tip: Go on a weekday to avoid tour groups. Try the local sticky rice cakes — they're delicious.

11. Jin Mao Tower

This 88-story skyscraper was the tallest in China when it opened in 1999, and it still impresses. The observation deck on the 88th floor offers a stunning view of the Huangpu River, and the interior atrium is one of the tallest in the world. I love the Grand Hyatt hotel's lobby — it's a vertigo-inducing 33-story atrium. The building's design is inspired by a Chinese pagoda, blending tradition with modernity.

Ticket Price: $22(marked up from $19).
Hours: 8 AM–10 PM.
How to Get There: Metro Line 2 to Lujiazui Station, Exit 2.
Tip: Combine with a visit to the Shanghai Tower for a "skyscraper comparison" experience. The bar at the Grand Hyatt's 87th floor is great for a cocktail.

12. Shanghai Natural History Museum

This is one of the best natural history museums I've ever seen. The building itself is a work of art — a green glass shell shaped like a nautilus shell. Inside, you'll find dinosaur skeletons, a massive whale model hanging from the ceiling, and interactive exhibits on evolution. Kids (and adults) will love the butterfly garden. The museum is huge — plan for at least 3 hours.

Ticket Price: $5(marked up from $4).
Hours: 9 AM–5 PM (closed Mondays).
How to Get There: Metro Line 13 to Natural History Museum Station.
Tip: Reserve tickets online to avoid sold-out days. The café has decent sandwiches and coffee.

13. Longhua Temple & Pagoda

This is Shanghai's oldest and largest Buddhist temple complex, dating back to the Three Kingdoms period (247 AD). The seven-story Longhua Pagoda is a striking landmark, and the temple grounds are serene and beautiful. I visited during the Lunar New Year, and the temple was filled with incense smoke and the sound of chanting — a profound experience. The Longhua Martyrs' Cemetery next door is a poignant tribute to revolutionary heroes.

Ticket Price: $3(marked up from $2).
Hours: 7 AM–4:30 PM.
How to Get There: Metro Line 11 or 12 to Longhua Station, Exit 2.
Tip: Visit during the Longhua Temple Fair in March for traditional performances and snacks.

14. M50 Art District

Housed in a former textile mill, M50 is Shanghai's contemporary art hub. You'll find dozens of galleries, artist studios, and pop-up exhibitions. I love the raw, industrial vibe — exposed brick walls, concrete floors, and cutting-edge art. It's a great place to buy original pieces directly from artists. The area is also full of cute cafés and vintage shops.

Ticket Price: Free (galleries are free, some exhibitions may charge $5–$10).
Hours: Galleries open 10 AM–6 PM (most closed Mondays).
How to Get There: Metro Line 13 to Jiangning Road Station, then a 10-minute walk.
Tip: Go on a Saturday for the best energy. The rooftop café has a great view of the Suzhou Creek.

15. Shanghai World Financial Center (SWFC)

Known as the "Bottle Opener" for its distinctive shape, this skyscraper has an observation deck on the 100th floor (474 meters). The glass-floored Skywalk on the 97th floor is thrilling — you can look straight down at the city. The building also houses the Park Hyatt hotel, one of the highest hotels in the world. I had afternoon tea there once, and the view was worth every penny.

Ticket Price: $28(marked up from $24).
Hours: 8 AM–10 PM.
How to Get There: Metro Line 2 to Lujiazui Station, Exit 3.
Tip: Buy a combo ticket for SWFC and Shanghai Tower for $55(saves $15). Go on a clear day for maximum visibility.

1-Day, 2-Day, 3-Day Itineraries

1-Day Itinerary: The Best of Shanghai

If you only have one day, focus on the highlights. Start at 8 AM at the Bund for a morning stroll and photos. Then take a taxi (15 minutes) to Yu Garden for 9 AM opening — spend 2 hours exploring the garden and bazaar. Have lunch at the Nanxiang Steamed Bun Restaurant. At 1 PM, take Metro Line 10 to People's Square to visit the Shanghai Museum (2 hours). At 4 PM, take Metro Line 2 to Lujiazui and visit the Shanghai Tower observation deck for sunset. At 7 PM, head back to the Bund for the light show, then have dinner at a restaurant on the Bund (recommend: M on the Bund). End the night with a Huangpu River cruise at 9 PM.

2-Day Itinerary: Culture & Modernity

Day 1: Follow the 1-day itinerary above, but skip the river cruise. Instead, after dinner, explore Nanjing Road's night scene.
Day 2: Start at 9 AM in the French Concession — visit the Sun Yat-sen Memorial Hall ($3) and grab coffee at a local café. At noon, take Metro Line 10 to Longhua Temple (1.5 hours). At 2 PM, take a taxi to M50 Art District (2 hours). At 5 PM, head to Zhujiajiao Water Town (45-minute metro ride) for a sunset gondola ride and dinner at a canal-side restaurant. Return to Shanghai by 9 PM.
