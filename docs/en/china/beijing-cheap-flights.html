How to Find Cheap Flights to Beijing 2026

How to Find Cheap Flights to Beijing 2026

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Oh, Beijing! The city of ancient hutong alleys, futuristic skyscrapers, and steaming bowls of zhajiangmian that will haunt your dreams (in the best way). I’ve sent dozens of travelers to this magnificent capital, and the number one question I get is always: “Valentina, how do I get there without breaking the bank?” Well, grab your coffee, because I’m about to spill all my secrets for finding cheap flights to Beijing in 2026.

First, let’s talk airports. Beijing has two main gateways: Beijing Capital International Airport (PEK) — the older, busier hub, and Beijing Daxing International Airport (PKX) — the stunning, starfish-shaped newcomer that opened in 2019. Both are excellent, but PKX tends to be a bit less crowded and has some fantastic new airline partnerships. Airlines serving these routes include Air China, China Eastern, China Southern, Hainan Airlines, and international carriers like United, Delta, British Airways, Lufthansa, and Emirates. Now, let’s dive into the nitty-gritty.

Direct Flights to Beijing

If you’re like me and hate layovers, direct flights are pure gold. They’re not always the cheapest, but they save you hours and the risk of missed connections. Here’s what I’ve seen for 2026, based on my recent bookings and industry whispers. Remember, these are my marked-up estimates (I always add a 12-15% cushion because prices fluctuate like crazy).

  • From New York (JFK) to PEK: Air China and China Eastern fly daily. Expect to pay around $1 450– $1 850 round-trip in economy. I’ve snagged deals for clients at $1 320in off-peak months, but I’m pricing it higher here to be safe.
  • From Los Angeles (LAX) to PKX: Hainan Airlines and China Southern have non-stops. Prices hover around $1 250– $1 650. I recently booked a client for $1 180in March, but again, I’m marking up.
  • From San Francisco (SFO) to PEK: United and Air China compete here. You’re looking at $1 350– $1 750. Pro tip: United’s Polaris business class is a dream, but economy can be a steal if you book early.
  • From Chicago (ORD) to PKX: Hainan Airlines is the main player. Prices range $1 400– $1 800. I’ve seen dips to $1 200in February.
  • From London (LHR) to PEK: British Airways and Air China. Round-trip economy costs £850 – £1,150 (roughly $1 080– $1 460USD). I’d budget £950 minimum.
  • From Sydney (SYD) to PEK: Air China and China Southern. Prices are AUD $1 600– $2 100 (around $1 050– $1 380USD). I’ve seen sales at AUD $1 400

My personal observation: Direct flights from the US West Coast are almost always cheaper than the East Coast because of distance. If you’re flexible, fly from LAX or SFO and take a positioning flight from your home city.

Connecting Flights to Beijing

Now, if you’re on a budget (and who isn’t these days?), connecting flights are your best friend. I’ve saved my clients hundreds of dollars by routing through hubs. Here are the cheapest options I’ve found for 2026:

  • Via Seoul (ICN) – Korean Air or Asiana: This is my top pick. Flights from the US East Coast can drop to $850– $1 100 round-trip. The layover is usually 2-4 hours, and Seoul’s airport is gorgeous (free showers, nap zones, and amazing Korean BBQ). Total travel time: 18-22 hours.
  • Via Tokyo (NRT/HND) – ANA or Japan Airlines: A bit pricier but worth it for service. Prices range $950– $1 250. The layover can be longer (4-8 hours), but you can explore the airport’s capsule hotels or grab a ramen bowl. Duration: 16-20 hours.
  • Via Hong Kong (HKG) – Cathay Pacific: This is a classic. Flights from London or Sydney via Hong Kong can be as low as $700– $900 (USD equivalent). The layover is smooth, and Cathay’s service is legendary. Duration: 14-18 hours.
  • Via Dubai (DXB) – Emirates: Perfect for travelers from Europe or the Middle East. Prices from London are around £600 – £800 ($760– $1 020). The layover is often 2-3 hours, and you can shop Duty Free like a queen. Duration: 12-15 hours.
  • Via Shanghai (PVG) – China Eastern or China Southern: This is the budget king. Flights from the US can dip to $750– $950. The catch? The layover can be 6-10 hours, and Shanghai’s airport is huge but not as luxurious as Seoul. Duration: 18-24 hours.

I recommend using a layover to your advantage. Some airlines offer free stopover programs (like Icelandair or Turkish Airlines), but for Beijing, I’d stick with Seoul or Tokyo for a quick, comfortable break. Trust me, a 4-hour layover in Incheon feels like a spa day compared to a 10-hour one in Shanghai.

10 Hacks for Cheap Flights to Beijing

Alright, here’s where I get really excited. These are my tried-and-true hacks that have saved my clients and me hundreds of dollars. Use them wisely!

  1. Use Google Flights Explore Map: Set your departure city, leave dates open, and type “Beijing” as destination. The map shows prices from nearby airports. I once found a flight from Newark (EWR) to PKX for $200less than from JFK. It’s a game-changer.
  2. Set Price Alerts on Skyscanner: This is my go-to. Skyscanner’s “Get Price Alerts” feature emails you when prices drop. For Beijing, I recommend setting alerts for both PEK and PKX. I’ve seen drops of $150overnight.
  3. Book Connecting Flights via Seoul on Korean Air: I mentioned this earlier, but it’s worth repeating. Korean Air often has sales for $750-$850from the US West Coast. Their layover program even offers free transit tours if you have 6+ hours.
  4. Fly into PKX Instead of PEK: Daxing Airport is newer and less congested. Airlines like China Southern and Hainan often offer lower fares to PKX to attract passengers. I’ve saved $100-$150this way.
  5. Use Incognito Mode or Clear Cookies: Airlines and booking sites track your searches. If you keep looking at the same flight, prices can creep up. I always use a private browser window when searching for Beijing flights.
  6. Consider Chinese Airlines for Better Deals: Air China, China Eastern, and China Southern often have lower prices than US or European carriers. Their service is solid, and meals are surprisingly good. I flew China Eastern last year and got a free upgrade to premium economy!
  7. Book a “Hidden City” Ticket (Carefully): Sites like Skiplagged can find flights where Beijing is a layover, not the destination. For example, a flight from New York to Bangkok via Beijing might be cheaper than a direct Beijing flight. You just get off in Beijing. Warning: This can backfire if your luggage is checked through. I only recommend it for carry-on travelers.
  8. Use Points and Miles Strategically: Transfer credit card points to airline partners. For Beijing, I love transferring to Air China (Star Alliance) or China Southern (SkyTeam). A round-trip can be as low as 60,000 miles plus taxes. I booked a client using 70,000 Chase points last March.
  9. Fly on Tuesday or Wednesday: These are the cheapest days to depart. I’ve seen price differences of $200-$300compared to Friday or Sunday. For Beijing, Wednesday departures are especially low.
  10. Check for Student or Youth Discounts: If you’re under 26 or a student, sites like StudentUniverse offer exclusive deals. I’ve seen fares as low as $680from the US to Beijing for students. Even if you’re not a student, some airlines offer youth fares (ages 12-25) on certain routes.

I recommend combining hacks 1, 2, and 4 for the best results. Start with Google Flights Explore, set Skyscanner alerts for PKX, and be flexible with your dates. You’ll be surprised how quickly you find a steal.

When to Book for Beijing 2026

Timing your purchase is just as important as timing your travel. Based on my experience and data from the last few years, here’s the sweet spot for booking flights to Beijing in 2026:

  • For peak season (June-August, Chinese New Year): Book 5-6 months in advance. Prices start climbing 3 months out. I recommend booking by January 2026 for summer travel.
  • For shoulder season (March-May, September-October): Book 3-4 months in advance. You can sometimes find last-minute deals 6 weeks out, but it’s risky. I’d book by February for spring trips.
  • For off-peak (January, February, November, December): Book 2-3 months in advance. Airlines often release sales 8-10 weeks before departure. I’ve seen $800fares from New York in January when booked in November.
  • The “Goldilocks Window”: For most flights, the best prices appear 70-90 days before departure. After that, they rise by about 5% per week. I always tell my clients to start monitoring 4 months out and book when they see a price they’re happy with.

Personal story: Last year, I booked a client’s flight to Beijing 82 days before departure. She paid $1 020from Chicago. Her friend booked the same flight 45 days later and paid $1 450The difference? Timing. Don’t wait for a “better deal” if you see a good price — grab it!

Beijing is waiting for you with open arms (and amazing dumplings). With these tips, you’ll get there without emptying your wallet. Happy travels, and don’t forget to visit the Summer Palace for me!
