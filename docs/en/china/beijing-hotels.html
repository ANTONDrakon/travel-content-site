Top 10 Hotels in Beijing 2026: Budget to Luxury

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Beijing in 2026 is a city that never stops evolving, and I’ve seen it firsthand during my last trip in early 2025. The hotel scene here is absolutely buzzing—from sleek new towers in the Central Business District to charmingly restored courtyard homes in the hutongs. The infrastructure is top-notch: most hotels now offer seamless digital check-ins, high-speed Wi-Fi that actually works (I’m talking 5G in your room), and smart room controls for lighting and temperature. Neighborhoods like Wangfujing, Sanlitun, and the area around the Forbidden City are packed with options, each with its own vibe. Prices have crept up a bit from pre-pandemic days, but you can still find incredible value if you know where to look. For 2026, I recommend budgeting around $45 to $55 for a solid budget stay, $65 to $170 for mid-range comfort, and $175 to $500-plus for true luxury. Remember, these are my curated rates—I always add a little cushion for the unexpected, like a sudden craving for Peking duck at 2 AM!

Let me walk you through my top picks for 2026, based on my personal travels and feedback from over 300 clients. I’ve stayed in most of these myself, and the rest come highly recommended by travelers I trust. Whether you’re a backpacker scrimping for street food or a splurger who wants a butler on speed dial. there’s a bed here with your name on it. I’ve marked up prices by 12-15% from what you’ll find on booking sites—because I believe in transparency. but also because I want you to budget for the real deal. including taxes and fees. Let’s dive in!

Budget Hotels (under $50/night)

If you’re watching your wallet, don’t worry—Beijing has some gems that won’t break the bank. These are perfect for solo travelers, students, or anyone who’d rather spend on dumplings and metro tickets than on a fancy lobby. Here are three I absolutely love:

1. The Hutong Hostel & Inn

This place is a dream for culture lovers. Tucked away in a traditional hutong near the Drum Tower, it’s a converted courtyard home with creaky wooden floors and a lovely shared garden. The vibe is super social—think backpackers swapping stories over instant noodles. Rooms are basic but clean, with shared bathrooms. I recommend the private double if you want a bit more privacy.

  • Price: $45 per night (double room, including breakfast)
  • Pros: Authentic Beijing experience, friendly staff, free walking tours
  • Cons: Thin walls, no elevator, shared bathrooms can get busy

2. City Comfort Inn (Tiananmen Branch)

Don’t let the name fool you—this is a no-frills chain hotel that’s all about location. It’s a 10-minute walk from the Forbidden City, and the rooms are compact but spotless. I stayed here once during a layover, and the blackout curtains were a lifesaver for jet lag. The breakfast is a simple buffet of congee and buns, but it’s filling.

  • Price: $48 per night (standard room, breakfast included)
  • Pros: Unbeatable location, 24-hour front desk, quiet at night
  • Cons: Small rooms, no gym, basic decor

3. 365 Inn (Sanlitun)

This hostel-hotel hybrid is a favorite among young travelers who want to be in the action. It’s in Sanlitun, so you’re steps from bars, clubs, and the best shopping. The dorms are cheap, but I recommend the private rooms—they’re tiny but have their own bathroom. The rooftop terrace is a highlight, with views of the city skyline.

  • Price: $42 per night (private room, no breakfast)
  • Pros: Lively neighborhood, great social scene, affordable private rooms
  • Cons: Noisy at night, thin walls, limited storage space

How to Choose the Right Area

Choosing where to stay in Beijing can make or break your trip. I always tell travelers to think about their main purpose. Here’s my breakdown:

For Sightseeing: You want to be near the Forbidden City, Tiananmen Square, and the hutongs. I recommend Wangfujing or Qianmen. The Emperor and City Comfort Inn are great here. You’ll be walking distance to history, but expect crowds.

For Nightlife: Head to Sanlitun or Gulou. Sanlitun is the party hub—bars, clubs, and late-night dumpling joints. 365 Inn and The Orchid are perfect. Just be prepared for noise until 2 AM.

For Business or Shopping: The Central Business District (CBD) or Financial Street are your best bets. Novotel and Ritz-Carlton are here. You’ll have easy access to malls and corporate offices, but it’s less charming.

For Relaxation: If you want peace, stay near the Summer Palace or the Olympic Park. Aman Summer Palace is ideal, or try a hotel in Haidian district. It’s a bit out of the way, but the tranquility is worth it.

Booking Tips

I’ve been booking hotels for years, and I’ve learned a few tricks to save you money and stress. First, book at least 3-4 weeks in advance for the best rates—especially in peak seasons like October (Golden Week) and May. I recommend using services like Booking.com or Agoda for mid-range options, but for luxury, go direct with the hotel—they often throw in perks like free breakfast or upgrades.

To save, consider booking a package with flights or using a travel agent (like me!). I can often get you rates 10-15% lower than public prices, even with my markup. Also, check for seasonal deals—Beijing hotels often have “winter warmers” from December to February with discounts of up to 30%. And always read the fine print on cancellation policies; some budget hotels are non-refundable.

Finally, my top tip: join loyalty programs. I’m a member of IHG Rewards and Marriott Bonvoy, and I’ve earned free nights just from my travels. Even if you only stay once, sign up—it’s free and you might get a welcome discount. For 2026, I’m seeing more hotels offer digital concierge services via WhatsApp, so ask about that when booking. Happy travels, and I hope you fall in love with Beijing like I did!
