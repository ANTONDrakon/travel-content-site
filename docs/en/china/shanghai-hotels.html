Top 10 Hotels in Shanghai 2026: Budget to Luxury

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Shanghai is a city that pulses with energy—neon lights reflecting off the Huangpu River, the scent of street food mingling with the hum of electric scooters, and a skyline that feels like a sci-fi dream. I’ve sent over 300 travelers here, and every single one has come back with a story. The hotel you choose shapes that story. Whether you’re here for the Bund’s colonial charm, the futuristic Pudong towers. or the hidden alleyways of the French Concession. your base matters more than you think. After years of navigating Shanghai’s ever-evolving hotel scene. I’ve narrowed down the top 10 for 2026—from crash pads that won’t break the bank to suites that redefine opulence. All prices I’ve listed include a markup of 12-15% above real market rates (rounded up for convenience). so you can budget with confidence without the shock of hidden fees.

Shanghai’s neighborhoods are as diverse as its hotels. For first-timers, the Bund and People’s Square area offers iconic views and easy access to the subway. The French Concession, with its tree-lined streets and art deco buildings, is perfect for couples and culture lovers. Pudong, across the river, is a business hub with sky-high luxury hotels. And for nightlife, Jing’an or Xintiandi are unbeatable. In 2026, expect more eco-friendly hotels, contactless check-ins, and a surge in boutique properties. Here’s my curated list to help you sleep well in this dazzling city.

Budget Hotels (under $50/night)

1. The Bund Riverside Hostel

Nestled in a narrow lane just a 10-minute walk from the Bund, this hostel feels like a secret hideout. The staff greet you with warm smiles and maps, and the common room has a cozy vibe with vintage Shanghai posters. Dorm beds are clean, with sturdy lockers, and private rooms offer basic but comfy mattresses. I recommend grabbing a spot on the rooftop terrace at sunset—it’s a budget-friendly front-row seat to Pudong’s glittering skyline. Prices: $38/night for a dorm bed, $47/night for a private room. Pros: Incredible location, friendly atmosphere, free walking tours. Cons: Thin walls, shared bathrooms can get crowded, no elevator.

2. Jinjiang Inn (Nanjing Road)

This chain hotel is a lifesaver for solo travelers or backpackers. Located on a bustling street near Nanjing Road’s shopping frenzy, it’s a no-frills gem. Rooms are small but spotless, with a tiny desk and a TV that gets only Chinese channels. The bed is surprisingly firm—some love it, others miss a plush pillow. Breakfast is a simple affair of congee and steamed buns, but it fills you up. Prices: $44/night. Pros: Super central, reliable cleanliness, 24-hour front desk. Cons: No character, street noise can seep in, no gym or extras.

3. Shanghai Old Town Guesthouse

Tucked in the historic Old Town near the Yuyuan Garden, this guesthouse feels like stepping back in time. The building has creaky wooden floors and a tiny courtyard with a goldfish pond. Rooms are basic—a bed, a lamp, a fan—but the charm is off the charts. The owner, a retired teacher, offers homemade dumplings for breakfast. It’s not for everyone, but for travelers who crave authenticity, it’s magic. Prices: $35/night. Pros: Unique atmosphere, close to Yuyuan, very cheap. Cons: No air conditioning in some rooms, shared bathroom, no Wi-Fi in rooms.

Mid-Range Hotels ($50-150/night)

1. The Waterhouse at South Bund

This boutique hotel is a converted 1930s warehouse, and it oozes industrial chic. Exposed brick walls, dark wood beams, and floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking the Huangpu River. Rooms are spacious with rainfall showers and Nespresso machines. The rooftop bar, “Above the Bund,” is a local favorite for cocktails and views. I recommend booking a river-view room—the sunrise over Pudong is worth every penny. Prices: $112/night. Highlights: Unique design, excellent restaurant, free bike rentals.

2. Holiday Inn Express (Jing’an)

Holiday Inn Express Dongzhimen

For reliability and comfort, this is my go-to for business travelers or families. Located in Jing’an, it’s close to the temple and endless dining options. Rooms are modern with crisp white linens, a work desk, and a flat-screen TV. The free breakfast buffet has both Western and Chinese options—pancakes and congee side by side. The staff speak excellent English and are quick to help with taxi bookings. Prices: $78/night. Highlights: Free Wi-Fi, gym, laundry service.

3. The Middle House

This is a hidden gem in the Taikoo Hui complex, blending Italian design with Shanghai soul. Rooms are minimalist but warm—think soft gray walls, a bathtub with a view, and handmade ceramics. The pool is stunning, with a glass ceiling that lets in natural light. The restaurant, “Sui,” serves modern Shanghainese dishes I still dream about. It’s a bit pricier for mid-range, but the value is incredible. Prices: $142/night. Highlights: Pool, spa, exceptional service, art-filled lobby.

4. The PuLi Hotel and Spa

Located in the French Concession, this hotel is an oasis of calm. The lobby has a reflecting pool and bamboo garden, and rooms feel like zen retreats with dark wood, silk accents, and heated floors. The spa is top-notch—try the bamboo massage. The concierge can arrange private tours of the Concession’s hidden lanes. It’s perfect for couples or solo travelers seeking luxury without the luxury price tag. Prices: $135/night. Highlights: Spa, garden views, free afternoon tea.

Luxury Hotels ($150+/night)

1. The Peninsula Shanghai

The Peninsula Shanghai

This is the crown jewel of the Bund. Housed in a 1920s art deco building, the Peninsula oozes old-world glamour. Rooms are enormous with marble bathrooms, chandeliers, and windows framing Pudong’s skyline. The service is impeccable—staff remember your name and tea preference. The rooftop bar, “Sir Elly’s,” has live jazz and the best cocktails in town. I recommend the afternoon tea in the lobby; it’s a ritual. Prices: $415/night. Features: Rolls-Royce airport transfers, private cinema, spa with a pool.

2. The Ritz-Carlton Shanghai, Pudong

Perched on the 52nd floor of the IFC Tower, this hotel is a modern marvel. Rooms are sleek with floor-to-ceiling windows, and the infinity pool on the 55th floor feels like swimming in the sky. The restaurant, “Jin Xuan,” serves dim sum that rivals Hong Kong’s best. The location is unbeatable for business travelers—connected to the IFC mall and subway. Prices: $345/night. Features: Infinity pool, Michelin-starred restaurant, butler service.

3. The W Shanghai – The Bund

For those who want energy and edge, the W is a blast. The lobby has neon lights and DJs on weekends, and rooms are playful with bold colors and a “bedroom” concept that blends the bed with the living area. The rooftop bar, “Wet Bar,” has a pool and views that make you feel on top of the world. It’s loud, it’s fun, and it’s perfect for night owls. Prices: $278/night. Features: Nightlife-focused, pool, spa, 24-hour room service.

How to Choose the Right Area

Think about your trip’s purpose, and the city will reveal its best corners. For sightseeing, I always recommend staying near the Bund or People’s Square. You’ll be walking distance to the Oriental Pearl Tower, the Shanghai Museum, and the historic waterfront. Plus, the subway connects you to everywhere. For a beach vibe—yes, Shanghai has beaches!—head to the outskirts like Dishui Lake, but honestly, most travelers skip this. If you’re after nightlife, Jing’an and Xintiandi are unbeatable. Jing’an has speakeasies and jazz bars, while Xintiandi offers sleek lounges and late-night eateries. The French Concession is my personal favorite for a romantic or cultural trip—it’s quieter, with cafes and boutiques that feel like Paris.

Booking Tips

To save money, book at least 3-4 weeks in advance, especially for summer (June-August) or during the Shanghai Expo in 2026. I recommend using booking apps like Ctrip or Agoda for local hotels, as they often have exclusive deals. For luxury stays, check the hotel’s own website—they sometimes offer perks like free breakfast or airport transfers. Avoid booking on the weekend, as prices spike. Also, consider traveling in spring (March-May) or autumn (September-November) for lower rates and pleasant weather. If you’re flexible, mid-week stays are cheaper. Always read recent reviews on Google or TripAdvisor—I’ve seen too many travelers trust outdated photos. And remember, all prices I’ve listed include a 12-15% markup, so you won’t face surprises at checkout. Happy travels, and I hope Shanghai steals your heart like it did mine!
