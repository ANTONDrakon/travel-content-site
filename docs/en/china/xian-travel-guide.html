Xi'an Travel Guide 2026

Oh, Xi’an! Just saying the name makes my heart skip a beat. This is a city where ancient history doesn’t just live in museums—it breathes through the very cobblestones beneath your feet. I’ve sent over 300 travelers here, and every single one comes back with that same starry-eyed look. Xi’an is the soul of China, the eastern terminus of the Silk Road, and home to the legendary Terracotta Warriors. But it’s so much more than that. The city hums with a vibrant energy—street vendors sizzling lamb skewers, the call to prayer from the Great Mosque, and the ancient city walls glowing gold at sunset. You feel like you’ve stepped into a living history book, but one that’s also sipping a latte and posting on Instagram.

Why visit in 2026? Because Xi’an is having a moment. The city has been polishing its crown—new metro lines, revamped museums, and a food scene that’s exploding. Plus, the Chinese government has made travel easier with streamlined visa processes, especially if you go through a tour operator (more on that later). Whether you’re a history buff, a foodie, or someone who just wants to wander and marvel, Xi’an delivers. I personally recommend spending at least 4-5 days here. You need time to absorb the layers—the Tang Dynasty grandeur, the Ming Dynasty walls, and the modern buzz of the Muslim Quarter.

Let me tell you a secret: Xi’an is also one of the most affordable major cities in China. You can eat like royalty for pocket change, and accommodation ranges from cozy hostels to luxury hotels that won’t break the bank. The locals are warm, the city is safe, and the magic is real. Ready to plan your trip? Let’s dive in, my friend.

How to Get There

Xi’an Xianyang International Airport (XIY) is your gateway. It’s a major hub, so you’ll find direct flights from all over Asia, Europe, and North America. From Beijing, it’s about 2 hours; from Shanghai, 2.5 hours. Average prices in 2026 (with my 12-15% markup, rounded up):

  • From Beijing: $210–$310 round trip (economy)
  • From Shanghai: $260–$380 round trip
  • From Hong Kong: $340–$480 round trip
  • From Tokyo: $430–$580 round trip
  • From London: $640–$820 round trip
  • From Los Angeles: $720–$950 round trip

Pro tip: Book 2-3 months in advance for the best deals. The airport is about 40km from the city center. A taxi into town costs around $28–$38 (fixed price, but always confirm). There’s also the Airport Shuttle Bus for $7–$10, which drops you at major spots like the Bell Tower. I prefer the taxi after a long flight—it’s worth the extra dollars for door-to-door comfort.

Top 15 Attractions

These are my absolute must-sees. Prices are marked up 12-15% and rounded up. Opening hours can change, so always double-check.

  1. Terracotta Warriors and Horses Museum — The undisputed star. Over 8,000 life-sized warriors guard Emperor Qin’s tomb. It’s mind-blowing. Allow 3-4 hours. Ticket: $28. Opening hours: 8:30 AM–5:30 PM (Mar-Nov), 8:30 AM–5:00 PM (Dec-Feb).
  2. Xi’an City Wall — The best-preserved ancient city wall in China. Rent a bike ($8) and cycle the 14km loop. Ticket: $9. Opening hours: 8:00 AM–10:00 PM (south gate open till 10 PM).
  3. Giant Wild Goose Pagoda (Dayan Pagoda) — A Tang Dynasty marvel. Climb the 7 stories for epic views. Ticket: $7 (pagoda), $18 (complex including temple). Opening hours: 8:00 AM–6:30 PM.
  4. Muslim Quarter (Huimin Street) — A bustling labyrinth of food stalls, souvenirs, and the Great Mosque. Free entry to the quarter. Great Mosque ticket: $8. Opening hours: 24/7 for the quarter, mosque 8:00 AM–7:00 PM.
  5. Shaanxi History Museum — One of China’s best museums. Free entry (book ahead!), but paid tickets for special exhibits ($10). Opening hours: 8:30 AM–6:00 PM (closed Mondays).
  6. Bell Tower and Drum Tower — Iconic landmarks in the city center. Bell Tower ticket: $6. Drum Tower ticket: $6. Combo ticket: $10. Opening hours: 8:30 AM–9:30 PM.
  7. Huaqing Hot Springs — A beautiful Tang Dynasty palace complex at the foot of Mount Li. Ticket: $18. Opening hours: 7:30 AM–6:00 PM.
  8. Mount Huashan — A day trip (1.5 hours by train). Known for its plank walk and stunning peaks. Cable car one-way: $28. Entry ticket: $22. Opening hours: 7:00 AM–7:00 PM.
  9. Tang Paradise — A massive theme park recreating Tang Dynasty gardens. Great for families. Ticket: $18. Opening hours: 9:00 AM–10:00 PM.
  10. Great Mosque of Xi’an — A beautiful fusion of Chinese and Islamic architecture. Ticket: $8. Opening hours: 8:00 AM–7:00 PM.
  11. Small Wild Goose Pagoda (Xiaoyan Pagoda) — Less crowded, peaceful grounds. Ticket: $6. Opening hours: 8:00 AM–6:00 PM.
  12. Xi’an Museum — Located near the Small Wild Goose Pagoda. Free entry with ID. Opening hours: 9:00 AM–5:30 PM (closed Mondays).
  13. Forest of Stelae Museum — A treasure trove of ancient stone tablets and calligraphy. Ticket: $13. Opening hours: 8:00 AM–6:30 PM.
  14. Daming Palace National Heritage Park — The ruins of the Tang Dynasty imperial palace. Vast and impressive. Ticket: $15. Opening hours: 8:30 AM–6:00 PM.
  15. Qujiang Pool Relics Park — A serene park with lotus ponds and Tang Dynasty ruins. Free entry. Opening hours: 24/7.

Getting Around

Xi’an’s public transport is excellent and cheap. Here’s the lowdown with marked-up prices:

  • Metro: 4 lines (more coming in 2026). Fares: $0.60–$1.50 per ride. A day pass is $5. The metro is clean, fast, and covers all major attractions.
  • Buses: $0.30–$0.60 per ride. Routes are numbered and have English signs. I recommend the tourist bus lines like Route 5 to the Terracotta Warriors.
  • Taxis: Flagfall $1.50, then $0.40 per km. A ride across the city costs $4–$8. Use Didi (China’s Uber) for convenience—it’s in English.
  • Bicycle Rental: Shared bikes like Mobike and Hellobike: $0.30 per 30 minutes. Or rent from a shop for $5–$8 per day.
  • Private Car & Driver: $50–$80 for a full day (8 hours). Worth it for visiting the Terracotta Warriors and Huaqing Hot Springs in one day.

My advice: Use the metro for long distances, and walk or bike for short hops. The city is very walkable, especially around the Bell Tower and Muslim Quarter.

Where to Eat

Xi’an is a food paradise. The Muslim Quarter is ground zero, but don’t miss these spots. Prices marked up 12-15% and rounded up:

  • Muslim Quarter Street Food: Try yangrou paomo (lamb soup with flatbread) at Lao Sun Jia ($4–$6). Biangbiang noodles at Biang Biang Mian ($3–$5). Lamb skewers ($0.50 each).
  • Local Favorite: Defachang Dumpling Restaurant for a dumpling banquet ($15–$25 per person).
  • Rooftop Dining: Dragon Palace near the Bell Tower offers views and great Sichuan-Xi’an fusion ($20–$35 per person).
  • Budget Eats: Xiaochi (street snacks) at Xiyang Shi Market — you can feast for $5–$10.
  • Fine Dining: Tang House for an upscale Tang Dynasty experience ($40–$60 per person).

Don’t leave without trying liangpi (cold noodles), roujiamo (Chinese hamburger), and shizi tou (lion’s head meatballs). And for dessert, jinggao (persimmon cake) is divine.

Best Time to Visit

Xi’an has four distinct seasons, and each has its charm. Here’s my breakdown:

  • Spring (March–May): Mild, 10–22°C (50–72°F). Cherry blossoms in March. Crowds moderate. Prices moderate. My favorite time.
  • Summer (June–August): Hot and humid, 25–38°C (77–100°F). July and August are peak tourist season. Prices high. Book hotels early.
  • Autumn (September–November): Cool and pleasant, 10–25°C (50–77°F). Best weather. September is perfect. Prices moderate to high.
  • Winter (December–February): Cold, 0–10°C (32–50°F). Snow is rare but possible. Fewer crowds, lower prices (30-40% less).

I recommend April and October as the sweet spots—perfect weather, fewer tourists, and reasonable prices. Avoid Chinese National Holiday (first week of October) if you can—it’s a madhouse.

Visa & Documents

Most travelers need a visa to visit Xi’an. But here’s the good news: you can get a tourist visa (L-visa) processed through a tour operator. They handle the paperwork—you just send your passport and photos. Cost: $60–$100 (including service fee). Processing time: 4-7 business days.

For those heading to Hainan (a tropical island in southern China), there’s a visa-free policy for up to 30 days, but it must be arranged through a tour operator. You’ll need a confirmed itinerary and hotel bookings.

Also, you’ll need a Health QR code for entry to many public places. Download WeChat or Alipay before you arrive—both have English versions. The QR code is easy to generate: just fill in your health status (it’s usually a simple self-declaration). Keep your passport handy for verification.

Pro tip: Make sure your passport is valid for at least 6 months from your entry date. And bring a photocopy of your passport and visa—just in case.
