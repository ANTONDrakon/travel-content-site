Haikou (Hainan) Travel Guide 2026

Imagine stepping off a plane into warm, tropical air that smells of salt and frangipani. That’s Haikou for you — the laid-back capital of Hainan Island, often overshadowed by its glitzy southern cousin Sanya, but honestly? I think it has more soul. Haikou is where old China meets new beach life: ancient banyan trees shade narrow streets lined with colonial arcades, while sleek shopping malls and seaside promenades hum with modern energy. It’s less crowded, more affordable, and feels delightfully authentic. I recommend spending at least two days here before heading south — you’ll fall in love with its unhurried rhythm.

Why visit in 2026? Hainan is China’s only tropical province, and Haikou is its cultural and economic heart. The city has been polishing its tourism game with new parks, upgraded beaches, and a growing food scene that’s a fusion of Cantonese, Southeast Asian, and local Li and Miao flavors. Plus, the visa-free policy for groups makes it a breeze to visit. Whether you’re a history buff, a beach bum, or a foodie, Haikou delivers without the tourist crowds. I’ve sent dozens of travelers here, and every single one came back raving about the relaxed vibe and the stunning Qilou Old Street.

In 2026, Haikou is also positioning itself as a hub for duty-free shopping and eco-tourism. The newly expanded Haikou Duty-Free Complex is a paradise for luxury lovers, while the volcanic geoparks and mangrove reserves offer genuine nature escapes. The city’s average temperature hovers around 24°C (75°F) year-round, making it a perfect winter sun destination. So pack light, bring your appetite, and get ready for a city that’s equal parts chill and charming.

How to Get There

Haikou Meilan International Airport (HAK) is your gateway. It’s well-connected to major Chinese cities and a handful of international hubs. From Beijing, direct flights take about 3.5 hours and cost around ¥1,200–¥1,800 (round trip). From Shanghai, expect 2.5 hours and ¥1,000–¥1,500. From Guangzhou, it’s a quick 1-hour hop for ¥400–¥700. International routes from Bangkok, Singapore, and Kuala Lumpur are also available, with prices starting at ¥1,500–¥2,500 round trip. I recommend booking in advance for 2026 — summer and Chinese New Year see price spikes of 20-30%.

Where to Stay

Haikou’s neighborhoods each have their own flavor. For first-timers, I suggest staying near the city center (Guoxing Avenue) or the Holiday Beach area. Here’s my breakdown:

  • Budget (¥150–¥350/night): Haikou Backpackers Hostel (clean dorms, social vibe, near Qilou) or 7 Days Inn (reliable chain, basic but comfy).
  • Mid-Range (¥400–¥800/night): Hainan International Hotel (great location, pool, breakfast included) or Grand Soluxe Hotel (modern rooms, close to shopping).
  • Luxury (¥900–¥1,800/night): The Ritz-Carlton Haikou (stunning ocean views, world-class spa) or Haikou Marriott Hotel (beachfront, huge pool, kid-friendly).

Pro tip: Book early for Chinese holidays (May Day, National Day) when prices double.

Top 15 Attractions

Here are my must-see spots in Haikou. I’ve visited every single one, and these are the ones that truly shine.

  1. Qilou Old Street (骑楼老街) — A living museum of 1920s arcaded buildings, blending European and Chinese styles. Wander the alleys, sip coffee, and snap photos. Free entry. Open 24/7.
  2. Holiday Beach (假日海滩) — Haikou’s main beach with soft sand, calm waters, and beachside bars. Perfect for sunset strolls. Free. Open 24/7.
  3. Volcanic Cluster Global Geopark (火山口地质公园) — Hike up an extinct volcano, explore lava tunnels, and see crater views. Ticket: ¥85 (adults). Open 8:00–18:00.
  4. Hainan Tropical Wildlife Park (海南热带野生动植物园) — A safari-style zoo with lions, giraffes, and tropical birds. Kids love it. Ticket: ¥120. Open 8:30–17:30.
  5. Wugong Temple (五公祠) — A historic temple complex honoring five Tang and Song dynasty officials. Beautiful gardens and ancient architecture. Ticket: ¥25. Open 8:00–17:30.
  6. Haikou Clock Tower (海口钟楼) — Iconic red-brick tower on the river, built in 1929. Great photo op. Free. Open 24/7.
  7. Evergreen Park (万绿园) — A massive urban park with tropical plants, jogging paths, and a lake. Locals fly kites here on weekends. Free. Open 24/7.
  8. Hainan Museum (海南省博物馆) — Learn about Hainan’s history, from ancient Li culture to maritime trade. Free entry (ID required). Open 9:00–17:00 (closed Mondays).
  9. Dongzhai Harbor Mangrove Reserve (东寨港红树林) — Take a boat tour through lush mangrove forests, spotting birds and crabs. Ticket: ¥50 (boat ride). Open 8:00–17:30.
  10. Haikou Duty-Free Complex (海口免税城) — A shopper’s paradise with luxury brands, cosmetics, and electronics. Duty-free savings of 10-30%. Free entry. Open 10:00–22:00.
  11. Binhai Avenue Waterfront (滨海大道) — A scenic coastal road with palm trees, modern sculptures, and ocean views. Great for cycling. Free. Open 24/7.
  12. Fenghuang Island (凤凰岛) — A man-made island with a lighthouse, luxury hotels, and a promenade. Best at sunset. Free. Open 24/7.
  13. Haikou People’s Park (海口人民公园) — A central green space with a lake, pavilions, and morning tai chi groups. Free. Open 6:00–22:00.
  14. Lingshui Li Village (陵水黎族村) — A cultural village showcasing Li ethnic minority traditions, weaving, and dance. Ticket: ¥60. Open 8:30–17:00.
  15. Meilan Airport Viewing Platform (美兰机场观景台) — Watch planes take off and land from a rooftop deck. Free. Open 8:00–20:00.

Getting Around

Haikou’s public transport is efficient and cheap. Here’s how to navigate:

  • Metro: Two lines (Line 1 and Line 2) cover the city center and airport. Fares: ¥2–¥8 per ride. Runs 6:00–23:00.
  • Buses: Extensive network with over 100 routes. Fares: ¥1–¥5. Pay with cash or transportation card. I recommend downloading the “Haikou Bus” app.
  • Taxis: Flagfall starts at ¥10, then ¥2 per km. A ride from the airport to city center costs about ¥60–¥80. Use Didi (China’s Uber) for convenience.
  • Rental: E-bike rentals are popular — ¥30–¥50 per day. For cars, expect ¥150–¥300/day. International driving permits are not recognized; you’ll need a Chinese license.

Where to Eat

Haikou’s food scene is a treasure trove. I’ve eaten my way through the city, and these are the highlights:

  • Street Food: Try Wenchang chicken (poached, tender), Hainan noodles (rice noodles in broth), and coconut rice. Prices: ¥10–¥30 per dish.
  • Restaurants: Yipin Seafood Restaurant (fresh catch, near Holiday Beach) — ¥80–¥150 per person. Old Dad’s Tea House (traditional dim sum) — ¥40–¥70 per person.
  • Local Cuisine: Don’t miss Hainan chicken rice (a must-try) and coconut chicken hotpot. For dessert, mango sticky rice and coconut pudding.
  • Markets: Dongmen Market for fresh fruit and snacks. Haikou Night Market on Binhai Road — open 18:00–24:00, with skewers, seafood, and cold beer.

Price ranges: Street food ¥10–¥40, casual restaurants ¥50–¥120, fine dining ¥150–¥300 per person.

Best Time to Visit

Haikou has a tropical monsoon climate. Here’s the monthly breakdown:

  • November to March: Peak season — dry, sunny, 18–25°C. Perfect for beach days. Prices are 20-30% higher. I recommend booking 2 months ahead.
  • April to June: Shoulder season — hot (25–32°C) and humid, with occasional rain. Fewer crowds, lower prices. Great for budget travelers.
  • July to October: Rainy season — typhoons possible, high humidity (30–35°C). Lowest prices, but outdoor activities may be limited. Avoid if you’re not a fan of rain.

My personal favorite? December — the weather is glorious, and the city is decorated for Christmas and New Year. Just book early!

Visa & Documents

For most nationalities, a visa is required for mainland China, but Hainan has a special visa-free policy. Here’s the deal:

  • Visa-Free: Citizens of 59 countries (including US, UK, Canada, Australia, most of EU) can stay up to 30 days visa-free, provided you arrive via a tour operator. You must book a package through a registered travel agency (like me!).
  • Health QR Code: Required for entry. Download WeChat or Alipay before you go. The code shows your health status and is scanned at airports, hotels, and attractions.
  • Documents: Valid passport (6+ months), return ticket, hotel booking confirmation. No need for bank statements or invitation letters.

10 Travel Tips

  1. Learn basic Mandarin: “Nǐ hǎo” (hello), “Xièxiè” (thank you), and “Duōshao qián?” (how much?) will get you far. English is limited outside tourist spots.
  2. Download WeChat and Alipay: China is cashless. You’ll need these apps for paying, booking, and the health QR code. Set them up before arrival.
  3. Stay hydrated: The tropics are humid. Carry a reusable water bottle — tap water is not safe, but hotels and shops sell cheap bottled water (¥2–¥5).
  4. Bargain at markets: At Dongmen Market or night markets, start at 50% of the asking price and negotiate politely. It’s expected.
  5. Use sunscreen and mosquito repellent: The sun is strong year-round, and mosquitoes are active at dusk. SPF 50 and DEET-free repellent are your friends.
  6. Respect local customs: Remove shoes before entering homes, don’t point with your finger, and avoid discussing politics. The locals are warm and welcoming.
  7. Try the local coffee: Haikou has a growing coffee culture. Visit a “tea house” (actually coffee shops) for strong, sweet Hainan coffee — a remnant of colonial times.
  8. Plan for typhoon season: If visiting July–October, check the weather daily. Typhoons can disrupt flights and outdoor plans. Have a backup indoor itinerary.
  9. Get a local SIM card: China Mobile or China Unicom offer tourist SIMs with data (¥50–¥100 for 7 days). You’ll need a Chinese number for Didi and food delivery apps.
