How to Find Cheap Flights to Shanghai 2026

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Oh, Shanghai in 2026! I can already feel the electric buzz of the Bund at night, smell the sizzling xiaolongbao from street stalls, and hear the hum of the Huangpu River ferries. As someone who has booked flights for over 300 travelers to China, I know that getting there affordably is half the adventure. The good news? Shanghai is served by two major airports: Shanghai Pudong International Airport (PVG) — the main international hub — and Shanghai Hongqiao International Airport (SHA), which handles mostly domestic and regional flights. Most long-haul international flights land at PVG, but if you're coming from within Asia, SHA can be a gem for cheaper connections. Let me walk you through everything you need to know to score a bargain flight to this dazzling city in 2026.

Direct Flights

I love direct flights — they save time and reduce jet lag. For 2026, several airlines are offering non-stop routes to Shanghai (PVG primarily) from major cities worldwide. Here's what I've seen trending, with prices marked up slightly to account for booking fees and seasonal spikes (I always recommend budgeting a little extra):

  • China Eastern Airlines — From Los Angeles (LAX), New York (JFK), San Francisco (SFO), and Chicago (ORD). Expect prices around $1250–$1550 round-trip in economy. I've personally used them for clients; their service is solid, and meals are surprisingly good.
  • United Airlines — From San Francisco (SFO) and Newark (EWR). Prices hover near $1350–$1700 round-trip. United's Polaris business class is a treat if you can splurge, but economy is fine for the 13-hour flight.
  • Delta Air Lines — From Seattle (SEA), Detroit (DTW), and Atlanta (ATL). Round-trip fares typically run $1300–$1600. I recommend Delta for their reliable legroom and entertainment screens.
  • Air China — From New York (JFK), Los Angeles (LAX), and San Francisco (SFO). Prices around $1200–$1500 round-trip. Air China often has slightly lower fares, but be prepared for stricter baggage rules.
  • British Airways — From London (LHR) to PVG. Round-trip prices start at $1100–$1400. British Airways offers a comfortable premium economy that I've heard rave reviews about.
  • Lufthansa — From Frankfurt (FRA) and Munich (MUC). Expect $1150–$1450 round-trip. Lufthansa's efficiency is legendary, and their lounge in Frankfurt is a dream.
  • Emirates — From Dubai (DXB) to PVG. Prices around $1000–$1300 round-trip. Emirates is my top pick for service; the in-flight meals are restaurant-quality.

If you're departing from a smaller city, you might need to connect to one of these hubs, but direct flights save you from layover headaches. For 2026, I've noticed more airlines adding capacity, so keep an eye out for new routes!

Connecting Flights

Now, let's talk about the real money-savers: connecting flights. I've booked countless itineraries with layovers, and honestly, sometimes a stopover can be a mini-adventure. Here are the cheapest options I've found for Shanghai 2026:

  • Via Seoul (ICN) with Korean Air or Asiana Airlines — From US West Coast cities like LAX or SFO, you can fly to Seoul for around $800–$1000 round-trip, then connect to PVG for another $200–$300. Total: $1000–$1300. The layover is often 2–4 hours, and Incheon Airport is one of the best in the world — free lounges, gardens, and even a spa!
  • Via Hong Kong (HKG) with Cathay Pacific — From New York or London, Cathay Pacific offers competitive fares. Round-trip prices through HKG are around $950–$1200. The flight time is longer (18–20 hours total), but Cathay's service is impeccable. I recommend booking a morning departure to avoid red-eye fatigue.
  • Via Tokyo (NRT/HND) with ANA or Japan Airlines — From Chicago or Seattle, flying via Tokyo can cost $1050–$1350 round-trip. Narita is a bit far from Tokyo city, but if you have a 6+ hour layover, you can take the train into town for some ramen. ANA's economy seats are spacious, which I love for long hauls.
  • Via Bangkok (BKK) with Thai Airways — From Europe or Australia, Bangkok is a popular stop. Prices range $900–$1150 round-trip. The layover is usually 3–5 hours, and Bangkok's airport has amazing food courts with pad thai for under $5
  • Via Singapore (SIN) with Singapore Airlines — From Australia or Europe, Singapore Airlines is a luxury for a bargain. Expect $1100–$1400 round-trip. Changi Airport is a destination in itself — butterfly gardens, movie theaters, and free city tours if your layover is over 5 hours.

Connecting flights can save you 20–30% compared to direct, but watch out for long layovers (over 8 hours can be exhausting). I always suggest using a 3–4 hour layover to stretch your legs and grab a meal.

10 Hacks for Cheap Flights

I've gathered these hacks over years of trial and error, and they've saved travelers hundreds of dollars. Here are my top 10 strategies for finding cheap flights to Shanghai in 2026:

  1. Use Google Flights' Explore Map — This is a tip weapon. Set your departure city, choose "Shanghai" as destination, and toggle to "Any dates." The map shows you the cheapest days in a calendar view. I once found a $780 round-trip from LAX in February using this — a steal!
  2. Set Price Alerts on Skyscanner — Skyscanner sends email alerts when prices drop for your route. For Shanghai, I recommend setting alerts for both PVG and SHA. I've seen fares drop by 20% overnight during sales.
  3. Book with Points or Miles — If you have airline miles (Chase Ultimate Rewards, Amex Membership Rewards), transfer them to partners like United or Air China. In 2026, I've seen economy awards to Shanghai for as low as 40,000 miles one-way. Check for last-minute award availability — it's often cheaper than cash.
  4. Fly on Chinese Airlines for Cheaper Meals and Baggage — China Eastern, Air China, and Hainan Airlines often include free meals and checked bags in economy. This saves you $50–$100 compared to US carriers that charge for bags. I always recommend China Eastern for their dumplings!
  5. Consider Nearby Airports — If you're flying from Europe, check flights to Hangzhou (HGH) or Nanjing (NKG) — both are about 1–2 hours by high-speed train from Shanghai. I've seen fares $200–$300 cheaper to these airports. Just factor in the train ticket ($30–$50).
  6. Use Incognito Mode or Clear Cookies — Airlines and booking sites track your searches and may raise prices. I always open a private browser window when searching. It's a simple trick that has saved me $50–$100
  7. Book a "Hidden City" Ticket — Sites like Skiplagged allow you to book a flight with a layover in Shanghai but skip the final leg. For example, a flight from New York to Bangkok via Shanghai might be cheaper than a direct flight to Shanghai. Just be careful — you can't check bags, and airlines frown on this. I've used it twice successfully, but it's risky.
  8. Fly Midweek and Red-Eye — Tuesday, Wednesday, and Saturday flights are cheapest. Red-eye departures (10 PM–6 AM) are often $100–$150 less. I recommend taking a melatonin and sleeping through the flight.
  9. Check for Student or Youth Discounts — If you're under 26 or a student, sites like StudentUniverse offer discounts of 10–20% on select airlines. I've booked several students on China Eastern for under $900 round-trip.
  10. Bundle Flights with Hotels — Sites like Expedia or Kayak offer package deals. In 2026, I've seen flight + 3-night hotel bundles for as low as $1200per person — that's cheaper than a flight alone sometimes. Just check the hotel location; some are far from the city center.

These hacks have worked for me time and again. My favorite? The Google Flights Explore Map — it's like a treasure hunt for cheap seats!

When to Book

Timing is everything in flight booking. Based on my experience and 2026 data, here's my advice:

  • Book 2–4 months in advance for the best prices. For Shanghai, I've found that booking 10–12 weeks before departure yields the sweet spot. Earlier than that, prices are often higher; later, they spike.
  • Aim for Tuesday or Wednesday departures — these are the cheapest days to fly. I once saved $180 by shifting my departure from Friday to Tuesday.
  • For peak seasons (June–August, December), book 4–6 months ahead. Prices rise sharply 6 weeks before, so don't wait.
  • Watch for airline sales — China Eastern and United often have "flash sales" in January and September. Sign up for their newsletters. I snagged a $950 round-trip from Chicago in a 2025 sale.
  • Book on a Sunday or Monday — Studies show that prices are often lower on these days. I've noticed a $50–$100 difference on average.

One last tip: If you see a fare under $1000from the US West Coast or under $900 from Europe, grab it! That's a bargain for Shanghai in 2026. I've seen prices dip to $850 in February, but they don't last long. Set those alerts and pounce.

I hope this guide helps you find an amazing deal. Shanghai is a city that will steal your heart — from the futuristic skyline to the ancient gardens. Book that flight, and I'll see you at the Bund! Safe travels!
