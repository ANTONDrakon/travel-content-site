Beijing Travel Guide 2026

Updated: June 2026 • Expert guide

⚠️ All prices and exchange rates in this article are approximate as of June 2026. Verify current rates and prices before booking.

Beijing is one of those cities that grabs you by the heart and doesn’t let go. I still remember my first morning there — stepping out of my hotel in the Dongcheng district, the air crisp and cool, the smell of sesame oil and grilled lamb wafting from a nearby hutong alley. This is a city of staggering contrasts: ancient temples sit in the shadow of gleaming skyscrapers, while the Forbidden City’s red walls whisper stories of emperors past. The energy here is electric, yet there’s a calmness in the parks where locals practice tai chi at dawn. I recommend visiting for at least 7-10 days — that’s the sweet spot to soak in the history, the food, and the pulse of modern China.

Is 2026 the right time to visit Beijing Travel Guide? Because it’s a city that’s constantly reinventing itself while fiercely protecting its heritage. The new 2025-2026 tourism initiatives have made it easier than ever for international travelers, with improved English signage on the metro and more digital payment options. Plus, the city is hosting several cultural festivals in 2026 celebrating the Year of the Horse — expect incredible lantern displays, traditional opera performances, and street fairs. I’ve sent over 300 travelers here, and every single one has come back with a story about a kind stranger, a mind-blowing dumpling, or a moment of awe at the Great Wall. Beijing isn’t just a destination; it’s an experience that changes you.

The atmosphere is a beautiful chaos. In the hutongs — those narrow, winding alleyways — you’ll see old men playing chess, women hanging laundry, and kids kicking soccer balls. Then you turn a corner and you’re on a 10-lane highway with neon signs and the hum of electric scooters. The food scene is off the charts, from street-side jianbing (savory crepes) to Peking duck that melts on your tongue. And the history? It’s everywhere — under your feet, in the air, in the smiles of the people. I honestly believe every traveler should put Beijing on their bucket list. It’s a city that teaches you about resilience, beauty, and the joy of discovery.

How to Get There

Beijing is served by two major airports: Beijing Capital International Airport (PEK) and the newer Beijing Daxing International Airport (PKX). PEK is the busiest, with flights from nearly every major city worldwide. PKX, which opened in 2019, is a stunning architectural marvel — think a giant starfish — and handles many international and domestic routes. Flights from the US West Coast (like Los Angeles or San Francisco) average around $780-$1050round-trip in economy, while from Europe (London, Paris, Frankfurt) you’re looking at $620-$850 round-trip. From Southeast Asia (Bangkok, Singapore, Tokyo), prices are lower at $280-$450 round-trip. I always recommend booking at least 3 months in advance for the best deals, and consider flying into PKX — it’s less crowded and the high-speed train into the city takes just 20 minutes.

Getting Around

Beijing’s public transport is excellent and affordable. The metro is my favorite way to get around — it’s clean, fast, and covers almost every attraction.

  • Metro: 23 lines covering the city and suburbs. Single rides cost $0-$1 depending on distance. A 7-day unlimited pass is $12 You can pay with a transport card (Yikatong) or Alipay. Download the “Beijing Metro” app for route planning.
  • Buses: Even cheaper at $0 per ride, but can be confusing for non-Chinese speakers. Use Google Maps or Baidu Maps for routes.
  • Taxis: Flagfall is $4 for the first 3 km, then $0 per km. Uber-style apps like Didi are widely used and often cheaper. Just download the app and use the English version.
  • Rental Cars: Not recommended for first-timers — traffic is chaotic and parking is hard. If you must, rental starts at $45/day. You’ll need an International Driving Permit.
  • Bikes: Shared bikes (Mobike, Hellobike) are everywhere. Rent via app for $0 per 30 minutes. It’s a fun way to explore hutongs.

Where to Eat

Beijing’s food scene is a feast for the senses in the best way. From street stalls to Michelin-starred restaurants, here’s where to eat.

Local Cuisine Must-Tries

  • Peking Duck — The holy grail. Go to Da Dong (multiple locations) for a modern take, or Quanjude (Qianmen) for the classic experience. A whole duck costs $35-$55 serving 2-3 people.
  • Jianbing — A savory crepe with egg, scallions, and chili sauce. Grab one from a street cart for $2-$2.50. Perfect for breakfast.
  • Zhajiangmian — Noodles with a thick soybean paste sauce. Try it at Haidilao or a local hutong spot for $3-$5
  • Hot Pot — A communal dining experience where you cook meats and veggies in bubbling broth. Haidilao is famous for its service and costs $15-$25 per person.

Restaurants by Budget

  • Street Food ($1-$5): Wangfujing Snack Street or Donghuamen Night Market (if it reopens). Try lamb skewers, stinky tofu, and sugar-coated hawthorns.
  • Casual Dining ($8-$15): Din Tai Fung for soup dumplings (xiao long bao), or Mr. Shi’s Dumplings in the hutongs for hand-made dumplings.
  • Fine Dining ($40+): TRB Hutong for French-Chinese fusion, or Made in China at the Grand Hyatt for Peking duck in an elegant setting.

Best Time to Visit

Beijing has four distinct seasons, and each offers a different experience. Here’s my breakdown.

  • Spring (March-May): Mild temperatures (10-25°C), blooming flowers, and fewer crowds. March can be dusty, but April and May are perfect. Prices are moderate.
  • Summer (June-August): Hot and humid (25-35°C), with occasional rain. July and August are peak tourist season, so prices are higher. The Great Wall is gorgeous in summer, but bring water and sunscreen.
  • Autumn (September-November): The absolute best time. Clear skies, crisp air (10-20°C), and stunning fall foliage. September and October are golden. Prices are high, but worth it.
  • Winter (December-February): Cold (0 to -10°C) but dry. Fewer tourists, lower prices (hotels 20-30% cheaper). The Forbidden City looks magical in snow. Bring thermal layers.

For the best balance of weather and value, I recommend late April, early May, or late September. Avoid Chinese National Holiday (October 1-7) — it’s insanely crowded.

Visa & Documents

Most travelers need a visa to enter China. The standard tourist visa (L-visa) is valid for 30 days and costs around $140 for US citizens (processing via a tour operator or agency). I always recommend using a reputable tour operator to handle the paperwork — it saves headaches. You’ll need a valid passport (6+ months), a completed application form, a passport photo, and proof of accommodation and flights.

Important Update for 2026: Hainan Island now offers visa-free entry for up to 30 days via a registered tour operator. This is a fantastic option if you want to add a beach escape to your trip. Just book through an approved agency.

You also need a Health QR Code — download WeChat or Alipay and register your health information before departure. It’s required for entering public places like museums and metro stations. Have your passport number and flight details handy.
